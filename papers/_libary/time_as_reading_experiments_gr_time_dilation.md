### Already Measured — Reinterpretation Available

**1. Pound-Rebka (1959).** Iron-57 gamma rays emitted at the bottom of a 22.5 m tower at Harvard, absorbed at the top. The frequency shifted by Δf/f = gh/c² = 2.46 × 10⁻¹⁵. Measured to 1%. Reading depth interpretation: 22.5 m of height is 22.5 m of reading depth difference within the Earth soliton. The gamma ray carries the emission depth's update rate. The absorber at the shallower depth reads a lower frequency because its own update rate is faster. The shift is the reading depth gradient of the Earth soliton over 22.5 m.

**2. Pound-Snider (1965).** Same apparatus, improved to 1% of the predicted shift. Confirmed GR to percent level. Reading depth: confirmed reading depth gradient to 1% over 22.5 m within a single soliton boundary.

**3. Hafele-Keating (1971).** Cesium clocks flown east and west on commercial aircraft, compared to ground clocks. Eastbound: −59 ± 10 ns. Westbound: +273 ± 7 ns. GR prediction (gravitational + velocity dilation): −40 ± 23 ns and +275 ± 21 ns. Reading depth: the aircraft clocks are at a shallower reading depth (higher altitude, faster update) but allocating update capacity to spatial displacement (velocity dilation). The east/west asymmetry comes from the Earth's rotation adding to or subtracting from the aircraft velocity relative to the non-rotating reading frame.

**4. GPS constellation (1978–present).** 31 satellites at 20,200 km altitude. Each satellite clock gains +45.85 μs/day from gravitational dilation (shallower depth, faster updates) and loses −7.21 μs/day from velocity dilation (orbital speed, capacity allocated to spatial displacement). Net: +38.64 μs/day. Corrected in firmware. Reading depth: the most continuously operating reading depth experiment in history. Every GPS fix is a reading depth calculation.

**5. Gravity Probe A (1976).** Hydrogen maser launched on a suborbital rocket to 10,000 km altitude. Measured gravitational redshift to 70 ppm agreement with GR. Reading depth: the maser's update rate increased as it rose to shallower depth, then decreased as it fell back. The trajectory traces a reading depth profile through the Earth soliton.

**6. ACES/PHARAO (planned ISS).** Cold cesium clock on the International Space Station compared to ground clocks via microwave link. Target: test GR gravitational redshift to 2 ppm. Reading depth: tests reading depth gradient from ground (deeper) to 400 km altitude (shallower) at 50× better precision than Gravity Probe A. The ISS orbits inside the Earth's Hill sphere — this is an intra-boundary test.

**7. Shapiro delay — Viking (1979).** Radio signals to the Viking Mars lander, passing near the Sun. Round-trip delay measured at 250 μs, matching GR to 0.1%. Reading depth: the signal passes through the Sun's soliton boundary (its deep gravitational reading zone). The photon's spatial update rate decreases while inside the deeper reading, taking more Planck steps to traverse the region. The 250 μs is the extra step count from the Sun's reading depth.

**8. Shapiro delay — Cassini (2003).** Radio signals to Cassini near solar conjunction. GR confirmed to 0.002%. Reading depth: same as Viking but 50× more precise. The most precise test of photon reading depth delay through a stellar soliton boundary.

**9. Pulsar timing — PSR B1913+16 (Hulse-Taylor, 1974–present).** Binary pulsar orbital period decreasing by 76.5 μs/year from gravitational wave emission. GR prediction matches to 0.2%. Reading depth: two neutron star solitons orbiting each other. Their reading depths oscillate as they approach and recede. The gravitational wave emission is reading depth energy radiated away. The orbital decay is the system settling toward a deeper combined reading (merger).

**10. Pulsar timing — double pulsar PSR J0737-3039 (2003–present).** Both neutron stars are pulsars. Each clock is visible. Relativistic effects (periastron advance, Shapiro delay, gravitational redshift, orbital decay) all measured independently. GR confirmed to 0.05%. Reading depth: two independent clocks at different and varying reading depths within a shared gravitational soliton. The most overconstrained reading depth test in astrophysics.

**11. Gravitational redshift — Sirius B (2005).** White dwarf companion of Sirius. Gravitational redshift measured from spectral lines: 89 km/s equivalent, matching GR prediction from the white dwarf mass and radius. Reading depth: photons emitted from deep within a compact stellar soliton boundary carry the deep reading's update rate. The redshift measures the reading depth of the white dwarf surface relative to Earth.

**12. Gravitational redshift — solar limb (1960s–present).** Spectral lines from the Sun's surface are redshifted by 636 m/s equivalent. Measured by multiple groups. Reading depth: the Sun's surface is at a specific reading depth within the solar soliton. The redshift measures this depth relative to Earth.

**17. Lunar Laser Ranging (1969–present).** Laser pulses bounced off retroreflectors on the Moon. Round-trip time measured to ~1 mm precision. **18. Binary white dwarf timing — LISA pathfinder/LISA (2030s).** The future LISA gravitational wave detector will observe binary white dwarfs with orbital periods of minutes. **22. Pioneer anomaly (resolved but instructive).** The Pioneer 10/11 spacecraft showed an anomalous sunward acceleration of ~8.7 × 10⁻¹⁰ m/s². **23. Voyager 1/2 at the heliopause (2012/2018–present).** The Voyagers crossed the heliopause — the Sun's magnetic soliton boundary — at ~120 AU. ### Cosmological Tests

**24. CMB acoustic peaks.** The CMB power spectrum has peaks at specific angular scales determined by the sound horizon at recombination. The sound horizon depends on the baryon-to-photon ratio (our η₁₀ = 6.090) and the expansion rate before recombination. Reading depth: the sound horizon is the distance that pressure waves traveled during the reading depth interval between Big Bang and recombination. The reading depth at recombination was much greater than today (the universe was denser, deeper soliton). The acoustic peaks encode the reading depth profile of the early universe. Test: does our η₁₀ produce the correct peak positions? This is already partially tested — our η₁₀ produces D/H at 0.12σ, and the same η₁₀ determines the peak positions.

**25. BAO (Baryon Acoustic Oscillations).** The BAO scale (~150 Mpc) is a standard ruler from the same sound horizon. Measured in galaxy surveys (SDSS, DESI). Reading depth: the BAO scale is a frozen reading depth oscillation — the distance at which the reading depth pressure wave was frozen at recombination, expanded to the present day. The BAO scale at different redshifts measures the reading depth expansion history. Test: does the BAO scale evolution with redshift match the reading depth model (which is identical to GR for smooth expansion but might differ at boundary transitions)?

**26. Type Ia supernova timing.** Supernovae at high redshift have lightcurves stretched by (1+z). This is standardly interpreted as cosmological time dilation — the supernova's internal clock runs at the reading depth of its emission epoch, and we observe it from our (shallower) current reading depth. The stretch factor (1+z) is the ratio of reading depths between emission and observation. Test: do supernova lightcurve stretches at z > 1 show any deviation from the pure (1+z) prediction? A deviation would indicate that the reading depth relationship between epochs is not purely linear in (1+z).

**27. Quasar variability time dilation.** Quasars at high redshift should vary more slowly by (1+z). This has been measured (Hawkins 2010 found possible deviations, Lewis & Brewer 2023 confirmed the (1+z) dilution in SDSS quasars). Reading depth: the quasar's internal variability clock runs at the reading depth of its cosmic epoch. We observe it from our current reading depth. The ratio is (1+z). Any deviation from (1+z) would indicate reading depth non-linearity with cosmic expansion.

**28. Hubble tension as reading depth differential.** Local H₀ = 73.04 ± 1.04 km/s/Mpc (SH0ES, Cepheids). CMB H₀ = 67.36 ± 0.54 km/s/Mpc (Planck). Tension: 5σ. Reading depth hypothesis: the local measurement is made inside the galactic toroid, at a specific reading depth. The CMB measurement is made from the cosmic boundary, at a shallower reading depth. The 8% difference is the reading depth correction between the galactic interior and the cosmic frame. Test: compute the reading depth ratio between galactic interior (inside the toroid) and cosmic frame (outside the toroid). If it equals 73.04/67.36 = 1.084, the Hubble tension is explained as a boundary reading effect.

**29. Cosmic clock test — aging of distant galaxies.** Jimenez & Loeb (2002) proposed measuring H(z) from the differential age of passively evolving galaxies at different redshifts. The "cosmic chronometer" method assumes stellar evolution clocks run at the standard rate at each epoch. Reading depth: stellar evolution is a reading depth process. At higher redshift (deeper cosmic reading depth), stellar clocks run slower. The differential aging should match (1+z) scaling. Any deviation tests whether cosmic reading depth scales linearly with expansion.

**31. Neutron star surface redshift.** Neutron stars have gravitational redshift z_g = (1 − 2GM/Rc²)⁻¹/² − 1 ≈ 0.2-0.35 depending on mass and radius. **33. Gravitational wave inspiral timing.** During the inspiral of a binary system, the gravitational wave frequency increases (chirps). The chirp rate encodes the system's orbital parameters. Test: does the chirp rate deviate from GR at any point during the inspiral? Current LIGO/Virgo measurements show no deviation to ~1%. Future detectors (LISA, Einstein Telescope) will test at 0.01%.

**34. Extreme mass ratio inspirals (LISA).** A stellar-mass object spiraling into a supermassive black hole. The signal persists for months-years, tracing thousands of orbits. ### The Key Distinction

Every experiment above tests the same thing: does GR time dilation fully account for the observed timing effects, or are there residuals that correlate with soliton boundary structure?

If reading depth IS GR time dilation (as conceded), then every experiment confirms reading depth. The question is whether the soliton boundary framing — galaxies as toroids, Hill spheres as boundaries, the nesting hierarchy — adds predictive content that the standard GR mass-distribution framing misses.

The two most likely places to find a difference:

**Experiment 28 (Hubble tension)** — the 5σ discrepancy between local and CMB H₀ is unexplained in standard GR. If it's a galactic toroid reading depth effect, the model explains a known anomaly.

**Experiment 39 (nuclear clock)** — if the nuclear reading depth (strong force) and atomic reading depth (electromagnetic force) respond differently to gravitational potential, this would prove that "time dilation" is not one effect but multiple boundary readings that happen to coincide in standard GR. This would be new physics visible only through the reading depth lens.
