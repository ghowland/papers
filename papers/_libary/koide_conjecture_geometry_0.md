## The Koide Relation

The Koide formula relates the three charged lepton masses:

K = (mₑ + mμ + mτ) / (√mₑ + √mμ + √mτ)² = 2/3

The parameterization that produces this uses:

mᵢ = M · (1 + a·cos(θᵢ))²

where M is an overall mass scale, the θᵢ are three phases equally spaced by 2π/3 (C₃ cyclic symmetry), and a is an amplitude parameter. Fitting to measured masses gives a² ≈ 2. If a² is exactly 2, the predicted tau mass is 1776.97 MeV versus measured 1776.86 MeV, a miss of 62 ppm.

The C₃ symmetry that produces the three equally-spaced phases is understood — it reflects three generations. The denominator's 3 is accounted for. The numerator's 2 is not.

## The Conjecture

RUM Chapter 1 establishes two stable soliton geometries as the building blocks of all structure:

- **Sphere** — the geometry of electrons, atoms, planets, stars. No preferred axis. Simplest stable pattern.
- **Toroid (donut)** — the geometry of protons, galaxies. Self-sustaining circulation through a hole. More complex stable pattern.

These are the only two stable soliton shapes identified in the framework. Every physical structure in the 12-layer soliton stack is built from spheres and toroids nested inside each other.

**The conjecture:** a² = 2 because 2 counts the number of stable soliton geometries. The Koide fraction K = 2/3 decomposes as:

- **2** = number of stable soliton shapes (sphere, toroid)
- **3** = number of generation modes per shape family

The charged leptons are three modes of the sphere soliton. The mass spacing between modes is constrained by the existence of the toroid as the other stable geometry. The amplitude parameter encodes the geometric relationship between the two possible patterns, not a property of the sphere alone.

## What This Would Mean Physically

Each charged lepton generation is a sphere soliton at a different energy level:

- Electron: Mode-1, lowest energy, permanent, 0.511 MeV
- Muon: Mode-2, higher energy, unstable, 105.658 MeV
- Tau: Mode-3, highest energy, unstable, 1776.86 MeV

The question the Koide formula answers is: given three modes, how are their masses spaced? The C₃ symmetry determines that the phases are equally spaced by 2π/3. But the amplitude — how far each mode deviates from the mean mass — is set by a² = 2.

If a² = 2 counts the soliton geometries, then the physical interpretation is: the mode spacing of sphere solitons is not determined by the sphere geometry alone. It is determined by the ratio of available stable geometries to available modes. The toroid's existence constrains the sphere's mode structure. Two geometries, three modes — the mass amplitude is their ratio.

This is analogous to how the existence of three color charges constrains the strong force running rate (b₃ = −7, where the 7 counts gluon self-interactions minus quark contributions). The strong force doesn't exist in isolation — its running depends on the full gauge group structure. Similarly, the sphere soliton's mode spacing wouldn't exist in isolation — it would depend on the full space of stable soliton geometries.

## Paths for Exploration

### Path 1: Topological Stability Analysis

**Question:** Can it be proven that exactly two soliton geometries are topologically stable in the quantum vacuum?

The sphere and the toroid are topologically distinct — a sphere has genus 0 (no holes), a toroid has genus 1 (one hole). Higher genus surfaces (genus 2, 3, ...) would represent solitons with two holes, three holes, etc.

**Test:** Determine whether the quantum vacuum's field structure permits stable solitons only at genus 0 and genus 1. If genus ≥ 2 solitons are topologically unstable (they decay to combinations of genus 0 and genus 1), then the count of 2 is forced by topology, not chosen. The integer 2 would be as determined as the integer 3 in SU(3).

**Method:** This requires the soliton boundary equation that Chapter 7 identifies as unwritten. The equation would describe self-sustaining patterns in the quantum vacuum. Its solutions would determine which topologies are stable. If only genus 0 and genus 1 have stable solutions, a² = 2 follows from topology.

**Difficulty:** Far. Requires new mathematical structure. This is the same soliton boundary equation needed to derive the electron mass from first principles — the deepest open problem in the framework.

### Path 2: Quark Sector Test

**Question:** Does the Koide relation extend to quarks, and if so, does a² = 2 persist?

The charged leptons are sphere solitons (three generations, same quantum numbers, different masses). The quarks are confinement-level vortices (three generations each for up-type and down-type, same quantum numbers within each type, different masses).

**Test:** Compute the Koide ratio K for up-type quarks (u, c, t) and for down-type quarks (d, s, b). If K = 2/3 with a² = 2 for quarks as well as leptons, the amplitude is universal across soliton types, strongly supporting the geometry-count interpretation. If K ≠ 2/3 for quarks, or if a² ≠ 2, the amplitude may be specific to sphere solitons and the geometry-count interpretation requires modification.

**Complication:** Quark masses are scheme-dependent (they depend on the energy scale at which they're defined) unlike charged lepton masses which are directly measurable. The Koide relation for quarks may hold only at a specific energy scale, which would itself be a finding.

**Difficulty:** Close. The quark masses are in the DATA-6 pool. The computation is one derivation function. This is testable in an afternoon.

### Path 3: Neutrino Sector Test

**Question:** Does the Koide relation hold for neutrinos?

Neutrino masses are known only as squared differences from oscillation experiments, not as absolute values. However, the Koide relation constrains the ratios, so it may be testable even with incomplete mass information.

**Test:** Determine whether the measured neutrino mass-squared differences are consistent with K = 2/3 and a² = 2. If yes, the relation is universal across all fermion types. If no, determine whether a different amplitude applies to neutrinos, and whether that amplitude has an integer interpretation.

**Difficulty:** Medium. Requires careful handling of the mass-squared difference constraints. The neutrino sector is identified in Chapter 7 as "far" from the current framework.

**Difficulty:** Medium to far. Requires understanding the relationship between soliton topology (genus count) and gauge group normalization (representation theory). These are currently separate mathematical structures.

### Path 5: Direct Numerical Test

### Second Reaction: The Argument Is Clean But the Logic Has a Gap

The conjecture says: 2 counts the stable soliton geometries (sphere and toroid). 3 counts the generation modes. K = 2/3 = geometries/modes.

The gap: the conjecture assumes that the numerator and denominator of K = 2/3 decompose into independent counts. But K = 2/3 is a derived ratio from the mass formula. The 2 and the 3 in K = 2/3 are not placed there independently — they emerge together from the structure K = (Σmᵢ)/(Σ√mᵢ)². The fact that this ratio equals 2/3 when a² = 2 is a mathematical identity of the parameterization, not an independent decomposition into "2 things divided by 3 things."

To make the conjecture rigorous, the argument needs to go the other direction: start from 2 stable geometries and 3 modes per geometry, derive the mass parameterization mᵢ = M(1 + a·cos(θᵢ))² with a² = geometry count, and show that K = geometry count / mode count follows necessarily. The current argument goes: K = 2/3, and separately a² = 2, and 2 and 3 are the geometry count and generation count. The link between a² appearing in the parameterization and a² counting geometries is asserted, not derived.

This is acknowledged in the conjecture — it is explicitly pre-M (no derivation). The gap is identified correctly. It does not invalidate the conjecture. It places it precisely where the document says it is: logic complete, empirical match exists, math not gated.
