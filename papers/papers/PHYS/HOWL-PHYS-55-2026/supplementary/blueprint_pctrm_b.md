## PART I: ONTOLOGY AND SUBSTRATE PRIMITIVES

### §1. The Ontological Commitment

Reality consists of a unit-adjacency graph on Planck cells that updates at Planck ticks through direction-conditional channel arithmetic on soliton-organized remainder flows.

This is the complete ontological claim. Nothing exists as primitive substrate beyond this statement. Solitons, channels, cells, ticks, remainder, and the hierarchy that organizes them are the fundamental constituents of reality. Everything observable emerges from the arithmetic of these primitives operating at each tick.

What does not exist as primitive:
- Continuous spacetime manifolds
- Mathematical singularities
- Infinite densities or energies
- Universal absolute coordinate systems
- Continuous fields existing independent of substrate activity
- A separate layer of reality beneath the substrate
- Privileged observer frames
- Separate measurement dynamics distinct from unitary evolution

What exists as primitive substrate:
- Cells: discrete spatial positions at Planck scale
- Ticks: discrete temporal advances at Planck time
- Direction-conditional adjacency: continuous direction, discrete distance
- Channels: edges between cells and solitons mediating remainder exchange
- Remainder: per-soliton accumulated vector quantity
- Solitons: bound patterns maintaining themselves through tick updates
- The hierarchy: nested structure organizing which solitons contain which others

All observable physics emerges as coarse-grained description of this substrate's arithmetic. The manifold physics uses for calculations is an emergent description that matches substrate behavior at measurement precision within each hierarchy position.

### §2. Substrate Primitives

**Cells.** Discrete spatial positions at the Planck scale. The number of cells in the universal soliton is finite but vast. Cells have no internal structure. Each cell has an identity (its position in the substrate graph) and a state (what remainder contributions currently affect it through channels).

Cells are the atomic units of position. There is no sub-cell spatial structure. A cell cannot be divided. Everything that happens at Planck scale happens between cells, not within them.

**Ticks.** Discrete temporal advances at the Planck time. The universal soliton advances by one tick at each step. All substrate operations happen at the tick boundary — per-tick remainder updates, channel activations, modulus crossings, soliton propagations, and merger events.

Between ticks, nothing happens. Time does not flow continuously. Each tick is the complete update of the substrate from one configuration to the next.

**Direction-Conditional Adjacency.** Each cell has neighbors at exactly one Planck-length distance in any continuous direction. Direction is continuous; distance is discrete and always equal to 1 Planck-length.

The substrate does not have a Cartesian axis structure. Cells are not arranged on a cubic lattice. "Adjacency" is defined by direction: for any direction you specify, there exists one neighbor at unit distance in that direction. Which cell counts as the neighbor depends on the direction being considered.

This means a photon moving in any direction advances one cell per tick, regardless of which direction is chosen. There are no preferred axes. The substrate is isotropic by construction.

**Channels.** Edges in the substrate graph connecting cells and solitons. Channels carry remainder between their endpoints at each tick. Channel activity can connect cells that are not spatially adjacent (entanglement channels connect solitons across arbitrary Euclidean separation).

Channel types include: gravitational (drain toward parent center), electromagnetic (between charged solitons), strong (confinement within nucleons, residual between nucleons), weak (interaction-specific), thermal (omnidirectional between thermally-coupled solitons), Higgs (tick-cost for massive solitons), entanglement (channel-sharing between specific solitons), and vacuum baseline (substrate's always-on channel activity supplying remainder to all cells).

**Remainder.** Per-soliton accumulated vector quantity. Each soliton has a remainder that accumulates contributions from its channels each tick. The remainder has direction and magnitude. When the remainder reaches specific thresholds, the soliton advances one cell in the remainder's direction and the remainder is consumed.

Remainder has two structural components matching the dual-geometry decomposition: spherical sector (modulus, associated with β² angular integrations) and toroidal sector (remainder proper, Layer 2 associated with K(k) elliptic periods).

**Solitons.** Bound patterns of stable cell-tick-remainder accumulation. A soliton is a persistent configuration of cells carrying specific remainder values updated tick by tick by specific channel interactions, maintaining itself over time.

Solitons are the atomic units of individuated being at each hierarchy level. Electrons, protons, atoms, molecules, planets, stars, galaxies, and the observable universe are all solitons. Every soliton has boundaries (defining what is inside vs. outside it), internal structure (cells and channels organized within), and membership in the hierarchy (it has a parent and possibly children).

### §3. The Per-Tick Update Rule

Between ticks, the substrate does not change. At each tick boundary, the following operations occur simultaneously across the entire substrate:

1. **Vacuum baseline activity updates all cells.** Every cell receives baseline channel activity from its direction-conditional neighbors. This baseline is the substrate's always-on rate of channel operations. It provides the remainder supply from which all other operations draw.

2. **Soliton channel budgets are computed.** Each soliton calculates how much remainder it receives this tick from its various channel interactions: vacuum baseline (source), Higgs cost (if massive, this reduces propagation budget), parent drain (gravitational, directional toward parent center), EM channels (if charged, based on other charged solitons), other channel types as applicable.

3. **Soliton remainders update.** The net remainder per soliton is accumulated. Direction vectors are summed. Magnitudes are added.

4. **Solitons propagate.** Where remainders reach propagation thresholds, solitons advance one cell in the remainder's direction. The remainder is consumed by this advance. Photons (no Higgs cost) advance one cell every tick in their propagation direction.

5. **Channel mergers resolve.** Where channel activity leads to merger events (entanglement creation, measurement via observation-as-entanglement, etc.), the mergers complete.

6. **Boundary transitions resolve.** Where solitons cross parent hierarchy boundaries (Hill spheres, galactic boundaries, etc.), their coordinate system reassignment completes.

7. **Universal soliton evolves.** The outermost containing soliton's internal structure updates by one tick.

All of these happen as one composite operation at the tick boundary. There is no "during" the tick; each tick is an atomic state transition.

## PART II: THE SOLITON HIERARCHY

### §4. Hierarchy Structure

The substrate organizes solitons into a seven-level hierarchy (Levels 0 through 6), with each level characterized by scale and the type of physics operating at that scale. Each soliton belongs to exactly one level, defined by what it is and what contains it.

**Level 0: Substrate.** Planck-scale primitives. Individual cells, immediate channels, and the finest-grained soliton structures (quarks as individual substrate patterns, individual field quanta). Scale: 10⁻³⁵ m.

**Level 1: Subatomic.** Composite particles formed from Level 0 substrate patterns. Electrons, leptons, quarks, composite hadrons (when considered as subatomic entities). Scale: 10⁻¹⁸ m for quarks/leptons; 10⁻¹⁵ m for nucleons.

**Level 2: Atomic.** Atoms and ions. Bound systems of Level 1 entities. Electrons orbital around atomic nuclei. Scale: 10⁻¹⁰ m.

**Level 3: Nuclear.** Nucleons as bound systems; nuclei as multi-nucleon bound systems. The strong force operates here as channel-mediated interaction. Scale: 10⁻¹⁵ m (at the nuclear scale within the atomic structure).

**Level 4: Molecular.** Molecules, chemical bonds, crystal lattices. Multi-atomic systems bound by electromagnetic channels at atomic scale. Scale: 10⁻⁹ m to 10⁻⁸ m.

**Level 5: Macroscopic.** Macroscopic bodies, astrophysical objects excluding galactic-scale structures. Planets, stars, planetary systems, asteroid belts, comets. Scale: 10⁻³ m to 10¹³ m.

**Level 6: Cosmological.** Galaxies, galaxy clusters, the cosmic web, the observable universe. Scale: 10²¹ m to 10²⁶ m.

**The Universal Soliton.** The outermost containing soliton. All other solitons are contained within it. The universal soliton has no parent at the hierarchy level. Its internal structure is what we observe as cosmic phenomena. Its inner boundary is the cosmic microwave background.

Each level's scale is approximate; the boundaries are structural (based on which soliton contains which), not rigid scale cutoffs. The hierarchy is the nested structure of containment relationships.

### §5. Parent-Child Relationships

Every soliton (except the universal soliton) has exactly one direct parent: the soliton at the next higher level that contains it. Every soliton has zero or more direct children: the solitons at the next lower level it contains.

**Examples:**

- The Moon is the direct child of Earth (the Moon is contained within Earth's hierarchy)
- Earth is the direct child of the Sun (Earth is contained within the Sun's hierarchy)
- The Sun is the direct child of the Milky Way (the Sun is in Milky Way's hierarchy)
- The Milky Way is the direct child of the universal soliton

The Moon is NOT a direct child of the Sun. The Moon is contained within Earth, which is contained within the Sun. The Moon is the Sun's grandchild, not its child. This distinction is substrate-level meaningful: the Moon interacts with the Sun's hierarchy only through Earth's mediation, not directly.

**Siblings.** Solitons sharing a direct parent are siblings. Earth, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, etc. are siblings under the Sun's hierarchy. The Sun and other stars are siblings under the Milky Way's hierarchy.

**Hill Spheres and Hierarchy Boundaries.** The spatial region within which a soliton's drain dominance exceeds its parent's mediated effects is the soliton's hierarchy boundary. Objects within this boundary are that soliton's children. Objects outside are the parent's direct children (or the children of other siblings).

The Hill sphere is the standard astrodynamics term for this boundary at solar-system scales. The framework uses "soliton boundary" as the general term. A spacecraft within Earth's Hill sphere is Earth's child. A spacecraft outside Earth's Hill sphere and inside Sun's direct region is the Sun's child.

### §6. Tax Structure

Gravity is hierarchical, not universal pairwise. The N-body gravity of Newtonian physics is an emergent description that agrees at measurement precision with the hierarchical tax structure. At substrate level, the mechanism is:

Each soliton is taxed (drained of remainder) only by its direct parent. The parent applies negative remainder (drain vector) to each of its children, directed radially toward the parent's center of mass. This drain is the gravity the child experiences.

The Sun drains Earth directly. The Moon is drained by Earth, not the Sun. The Sun's influence on the Moon propagates through how the Sun drains Earth, which modulates Earth's drain on the Moon. Tidal effects arise this way: the Sun drains Earth differentially (different parts of Earth receive slightly different drain based on Sun-Earth geometry), and Earth's drain on its children (including oceans and Moon) is modulated accordingly.

**Sibling interactions.** Siblings in the same parent's hierarchy do not directly drain each other. Jupiter does not directly drain Saturn. Their interactions proceed through their shared parent (the Sun) and the drain structure the parent maintains. Jupiter's position affects the Sun's internal drain distribution, which propagates to Saturn; Saturn's position similarly affects distribution to Jupiter. The net effect is the observed perturbation pattern of N-body celestial mechanics.

At measurement precision, the hierarchical mechanism produces the same observable orbits as direct pairwise gravity. The mechanism is different; the numerical results agree.

**Implications for the N-body problem.** What is conventionally called N-body gravity is actually a hierarchical tree structure. In the solar system: Sun at root, planets as direct children, moons as children of planets, satellites as children of moons. Calculations performed as hierarchical tax produce the same observable physics.

## PART III: COORDINATE SYSTEMS AND POSITION

### §7. Position is Hierarchical

Position is not specified in a universal Cartesian coordinate system. Each parent provides its own coordinate system for its children.

A soliton's position is specified by:
1. Its parent (which hierarchy it belongs to)
2. Its running reading depth within the parent's structure
3. Its relative position to siblings in the same parent's hierarchy
4. Its angular position within the parent's structural coordinates

Earth's position is specified in the Sun's coordinate system: 1 AU from Sun-center, at a specific ecliptic longitude, in a specific orbital plane. The Sun's coordinate system doesn't include the Moon's position — the Moon is in Earth's coordinate system. The Moon's position is specified by Earth: ~384,400 km from Earth-center, at a specific angular position, in a specific orbital plane.

**No universal grid unifies these coordinate systems.** Each is local to a parent. The hierarchy of coordinate systems is the hierarchy of solitons. Observers embedded in the hierarchy (like us on Earth) use our local coordinate system to make measurements, but measurements across hierarchy boundaries require explicit composition through the hierarchy tree.

### §8. Running Reading Depth

Running reading depth is the depth coordinate within a parent soliton's structure. For spherical solitons, this is radial distance from center. For toroidal solitons, this is depth into the toroidal structure (inside-out or outside-in depending on measurement point).

**For spherical parent solitons:** Position includes radial depth (r), polar angle (θ), and azimuthal angle (φ). The Moon's position in Earth's hierarchy is (r ≈ 384,400 km, specific θ, specific φ at each moment). Earth's position in the Sun's hierarchy is (r ≈ 1 AU, specific ecliptic coordinates).

**For toroidal parent solitons (like galaxies):** Position includes:
- Depth into the toroidal structure (radial from the central axis)
- Angular position around the toroidal ring
- Vertical position relative to the disk plane

A star in the Milky Way has a specific depth in the galactic disk, an angular position around the galaxy's rotational axis, and a vertical offset from the disk plane.

**Within a hierarchy, distances and angles are well-defined.** Stars at the same galactic depth are equidistant from the Milky Way's axis. Their azimuthal positions are comparable (both measured around the galactic rotation axis). Their disk vertical offsets are comparable (both measured from the disk plane).

**Across hierarchies, direct comparison is invalid.** The Moon's position in Earth's coordinate system cannot be directly compared to another satellite's position in Mars's coordinate system. They're in different coordinate systems. Composition through the hierarchy is required: Moon → Earth → Sun → Mars → other satellite.

### §9. Planck Distances are Parent-Relative

Cell counts between solitons are local to their parent soliton's coordinate system. The Planck-scale distance between objects is a quantity that exists in the context of their shared parent, not in a universal grid.

**Valid direct comparisons:**
- Two children of the same parent (siblings): direct cell-count comparison valid
- A parent and its direct child: direct cell-count comparison valid
- Cells within the same parent's structure: direct comparison valid

**Invalid direct comparisons:**
- A child of one parent and a child of another parent: composition required
- Objects at different hierarchy levels: composition required
- Distances from the universal soliton to specific solitons: composition through hierarchy

**Example — Sun-to-Moon distance:**
The Moon is in Earth's hierarchy. The Sun is the Sun's own structure. The distance from Sun to Moon is composed:
- Sun to Earth: measured in Sun's coordinate system
- Earth to Moon: measured in Earth's coordinate system
- Potentially a boundary transition term at the interface

The composition yields what observers call "Sun-to-Moon distance." At measurement precision, this matches standard astronomy's calculation. At the substrate level, it's a composition of different-hierarchy distances, not a single straight-line measurement.

**Example — Earth-to-Mars distance:**
Both Earth and Mars are children of the Sun. Their distance is well-defined in the Sun's coordinate system. Direct comparison is valid.

### §10. Planck Quantities are Hierarchy-Local

The specific magnitudes of Planck length, Planck time, and derived quantities (G, ℏ, c in local units) vary with hierarchy position. This follows from the hierarchy-local coordinate commitment: each position has its own cell and tick structure reflecting local soliton configuration.

**What remains invariant:**
- Cell-per-tick speed: photons always advance 1 cell per 1 tick (substrate arithmetic)
- Unit-graph adjacency: every cell has neighbors at 1 Planck-length in any direction
- Channel-merger arithmetic: same mechanism everywhere
- Round-trip closure producing Born-rule structure: same everywhere
- The substrate's operational rules: invariant across all hierarchy positions

**What varies with hierarchy position:**
- Local Planck length in SI units (meters)
- Local Planck time in SI units (seconds)
- Local G (gravitational coupling)
- Local ℏ (quantum of action)
- Local c (measured in m/s) — because length and time units scale together

General relativity's gravitational time dilation (clocks at different gravitational potentials tick differently) is the observable signature of this hierarchy-local variation. The GPS system must account for this: clocks at satellite altitude tick faster than clocks on Earth's surface, measured in any consistent frame. The framework's commitment is that this is substrate-level — different hierarchy positions have different local tick durations.

**At current measurement precision, these differences are minimal.** The fractional variation between Earth's surface and low Earth orbit is on the order of 10⁻¹⁰. Within the solar system, variations are small. Between different galaxies, variations may be larger but remain within bounds consistent with observational constraints.

The framework's commitment: at every hierarchy position, local measurements of fundamental constants yield consistent values within that position. Cross-position comparisons require hierarchy-boundary composition.

### §11. Hierarchy Boundary Transitions

Spatial regions where a soliton's coordinate system yields to its parent's (or a sibling's) coordinate system are hierarchy boundaries. These transitions occur at Hill spheres and other soliton boundaries.

**Boundary behavior:**
- Inside a soliton's boundary: the soliton's coordinate system is primary
- At the boundary: coordinate systems compose or hand off
- Outside: the parent's coordinate system (or a sibling's) is primary

**For photons traversing boundaries:**
Photons propagate at 1 cell per tick regardless of which hierarchy's coordinate system they occupy. The cell-per-tick relation is substrate arithmetic, invariant. Light travel time across a boundary is the sum of travel times within each hierarchy the photon traverses.

Photons are not "taxed" by hierarchy parents (no Higgs coupling, no direct gravity in the mass sense). Their propagation is mediated by vacuum baseline activity at each cell. Hierarchy boundaries don't affect photon propagation at substrate level; they only affect the interpretation of where the photon "is" in a given coordinate system.

**For massive objects traversing boundaries:**
When an object moves through a Hill sphere or hierarchy boundary, its parent-child relationship changes. The drain structure affecting it changes from one parent's to another's. At the transition, the drain vectors are reconfigured.

In practice, at current spacecraft-tracking precision, this transition is sufficiently smooth that standard N-body calculations reproduce measured trajectories. The framework commits that the substrate-level transition is well-defined but its observable effects are below current measurement precision for most configurations.

Possible future signatures at improved precision:
- Timing effects as clocks cross boundaries
- Small perturbations in spacecraft trajectories at specific transition points
- Effects in gravitational wave propagation across cosmic hierarchy boundaries

### §12. Every Soliton Has Two Geometric Sectors

Every soliton at every hierarchy level has two geometric components: a spherical sector (the modulus) and a toroidal sector (the remainder). This is not metaphor or analogy; it is structural composition of the soliton's channel arithmetic.

**Spherical sector (modulus):** The soliton's angular operations on spherical subspaces of its channel structure. Contributions from this sector involve β² in L1/L2 framework (MATH-11). The spherical sector produces the soliton's basic boundary structure: confinement boundary for protons, orbital shells for atoms, gravitational boundary for planets, dark matter halo for galaxies.

**Toroidal sector (remainder):** The soliton's angular operations on topological tori within its channel structure. Contributions involve K(k) and E(k) elliptic periods (MATH-12). The toroidal sector produces the soliton's internal flow structure: gluon flux tubes for protons, magnetic moment for atoms, Van Allen belts for Earth, galactic disk for galaxies.

Both sectors are structurally present at every scale. What differs by scale and by probe wavelength is which sector dominates observation.

The spherical sector uses β = π/4 as the L1/L2 conversion factor. β converts between the L1 (rectilinear) and L2 (Euclidean) metrics on spherical subspaces. Every angular integration on a sphere contributes a β² factor.

**Exponent counting:**

- β⁴ counts two angular integrations
- β⁶ counts three angular integrations
- β^(2n) counts n angular integrations

This exponent counting operates at quantum electrodynamics precision (PHYS-49 documents it at parts-per-million to parts-per-billion precision in four-loop coefficients). The same exponent counting appears in cosmological partition structure and in all spherical sector calculations.

**Applications across hierarchy:**

### §14. MATH-12 Toroidal Sector Structure

The toroidal sector uses complete elliptic integrals K(k) and E(k), and combinations thereof, as the basic conversion factors. K(k) is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind (the elliptic period). E(k) is of the second kind (the arc length).

**Toroidal conversion factor:** K(k)/π is analogous to β for spherical sector. It converts between toroidal topological structure and the circular (standard) reference.

## PART V: CHANNELS AND INTERACTIONS

### §17. Channel Type Catalog

The substrate supports the following channel types, each with characteristic activation conditions, directional structure, and function:

**Vacuum Baseline Channels.** Always active. Connect cells to their direction-conditional neighbors. Carry the substrate's baseline channel activity. Provide the per-tick remainder supply from which all other channels draw. Non-directional at rest; receive directional bias near parent solitons.

**Gravitational (Drain) Channels.** Always active (for massive solitons). Connect parent solitons to their children. Directional: drain vector points radially from child toward parent's center. Throughput proportional to parent's drain-producing structure. Operates at all hierarchy levels. Mediates what we observe as gravity.

**Electromagnetic Channels.** Always active between charged solitons. Connect charged solitons pairwise. Directional: continuous vector aligned with the inter-soliton direction. Throughput proportional to product of charges divided by distance squared. Operates at Level 0-6. Mediates electromagnetic interactions.

**Strong (Confinement) Channels.** Always active within nucleon boundary. Connect quarks within a nucleon. Directional: toroidal flux tube structure. Throughput extremely high; operates as confinement. Operates primarily at Level 3 (nuclear scale). Provides 99% of nucleon mass.

**Strong (Residual) Channels.** Active between nucleons within binding range. Connect protons and neutrons in nuclei. Directional: toroidal residual interaction. Throughput moderate. Operates at Level 3. Produces nuclear binding energies.

**Weak Channels.** Conditional on specific interaction events. Connect solitons undergoing flavor-changing or helicity-changing events. Per-interaction throughput. Operates at Level 1-3. Mediates decay, neutrino oscillation, flavor-changing processes.

**Thermal Channels.** Always active between thermally-coupled solitons. Connect solitons at similar temperatures. Directional: omnidirectional random. Throughput temperature-dependent. Operates at all hierarchy levels where thermodynamics applies. Mediates thermal contact, equilibration, decoherence contribution.

**Higgs Channels.** Always active for massive solitons. Connect massive solitons to Higgs-field-like substrate structure. Directional: produces tick-cost per propagation cycle. Throughput proportional to Higgs-coupling (mass). Operates at Level 1-6 for massive matter. Mediates what we observe as mass.

**Entanglement (Channel-Sharing) Channels.** Conditional on specific creation events (decay, scattering, parametric down-conversion, laboratory preparation). Connect solitons that have merged channel substrate. Directional: pattern-global across all soliton endpoints. Throughput preserves correlation but not energy/signaling. Operates at all hierarchy levels where quantum mechanics is relevant. Mediates entanglement.

### §18. Channel Sharing as Entanglement Mechanism

Entangled solitons share channel substrate. This is not correlation between two separate solitons linked by a channel. It is solitons that have merged their channel structure into one pattern with multiple Euclidean handles.

**What channel-sharing means substrate-level:**
Two solitons that undergo an entanglement-creating interaction merge their channel structures. Their combined pattern has two (or more) Euclidean expressions — one at each soliton endpoint. From outside, the observer sees two separate objects at different Euclidean positions. From the substrate, it's one soliton with multiple handles.

**This is not:**
- Two separate solitons connected by an "entanglement channel"
- Instantaneous signaling between two particles
- A hidden variable correlated between two particles
- A coordinated behavior across separate objects

**This is:**
- One merged soliton pattern with distributed Euclidean presence
- Graph-level adjacency between cells that are Euclidean-distant
- Substrate-level non-locality that is structurally local (graph-adjacent)

**Implications:**
- No-signaling holds: symmetric merger provides no sender-receiver asymmetry, so no directional information can be transmitted
- Distance-independence: the merger doesn't traverse Euclidean space, so distance doesn't affect correlation
- Persistence: channel-sharing persists as long as solitons maintain their merger (until measurement or environmental decoherence)
- Monogamy: each soliton can share channels with limited number of partners (substrate capacity constraint)

### §19. Channel Persistence

Entanglement channels persist with the solitons that share them. As solitons move through cells, the shared channel structure moves with them. This is how entanglement can persist across arbitrary distances: the channel is attached to the solitons, not to specific cells in space.

**Creation events:**
- Decay with correlated final state
- Scattering with entangled output
- Interaction through shared coupling producing entanglement
- Engineered laboratory preparation (parametric down-conversion, beam-splitter configurations, spontaneous emission with constraint)
- Spin-orbit coupling producing entangled internal states

**Persistence:**
- Duration limited by decoherence timescale (environmental channel-merger competition)
- Duration limited by measurement events (observer channel-merger dominance)

- Not limited by time absent interaction (can persist indefinitely if isolated)

**Termination events:**

- Decoherence: environmental channel-merger outcompetes prior shared channel
- Interaction rearrangement: other channel events displace the shared channel
- All terminate through the same substrate mechanism: a stronger channel-merger displaces the weaker

## PART VI: VACUUM AND REMAINDER SUPPLY

### §20. Vacuum as Substrate Baseline Activity

The vacuum is the substrate's always-on channel activity. Between solitons, cells are still updating each tick. Channel activity happens whether or not any stable soliton is present. That baseline activity, independent of specific solitons, is the vacuum.

**The vacuum is not empty.** It's the baseline rate at which cells exchange remainder through their direction-conditional neighbors. It's channel activity without soliton participation. It's the substrate doing its intrinsic operations.

**The vacuum is not a background field.** In standard QFT, vacuum fluctuations are excitations of quantum fields. In PCTRM, the vacuum is not a field — it's the substrate's own channel operations. There's no "field" below the substrate; the substrate itself is doing the activity.

**Vacuum activity is not random.** The substrate performs definite channel arithmetic at each tick. However, from the perspective of any individual soliton, the vacuum's contributions to its cells can appear stochastic because the substrate-level details are at Planck scale below any observer's resolution.

**Vacuum baseline rate varies with hierarchy position.** The local baseline depends on the parent soliton's structure and the hierarchy's configuration at that position. This is why fundamental constants have hierarchy-local values — the baseline vacuum activity rate is the substrate-level origin of those constants' measured magnitudes.

### §21. Per-Tick Remainder Budget

Every soliton draws from local vacuum baseline activity for its per-tick remainder budget. This budget is spent on propagation plus whatever channel interactions the soliton participates in.

**Budget allocation:**
- **Propagation:** remainder spent advancing the soliton's position by one cell in the remainder's direction
- **Higgs interactions:** remainder spent maintaining Higgs coupling (mass-dependent)
- **Gravitational drain:** remainder spent on drain-receiving (depends on parent's drain structure)
- **Electromagnetic channels:** remainder spent on EM interactions (depends on charge configuration)
- **Other channel types:** remainder spent on strong, weak, thermal, entanglement, etc.

**Photons:** No Higgs coupling, no gravitational drain in mass sense (direction-drift only), no EM interaction (photons are EM, not receivers), limited other channels. Full budget goes to propagation. Hence 1 cell per 1 tick.

**Massive particles:** Budget split across propagation, Higgs maintenance, and other channels. Propagation budget less than full. Hence sub-c velocity.

**Relativistic particles:** Approaching c requires shifting more budget toward propagation and less toward other channels. As velocity approaches c, Higgs budget and other costs become relatively smaller fractions of the total. In the limit, a massive particle cannot reach c because Higgs budget would need to vanish.

### §22. Vacuum Fluctuations as Baseline Variance

Not every tick delivers identical baseline to every cell. The substrate's channel activity has inherent cell-to-cell variance. This variance is what we observe as quantum fluctuations.

**Structural grounding of uncertainty:**
- Position-localization requires extracting remainder from a small region (variance matters there)
- Momentum-definition requires extracting remainder from many cells (variance averages)
- The position-momentum tradeoff is structural: you can't simultaneously have sharp position (small spatial region) and sharp momentum (averaged direction) because they draw from different amounts of vacuum contribution
