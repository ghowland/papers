The Titius-Bode law is one of the oldest pattern observations in astronomy and one of the most contentious. Let me lay out what it actually says, where it works, where it fails, and then what it looks like through the soliton mode lens.

**The law itself.** In 1766, Johann Daniel Titius noticed that if you write the sequence 0, 3, 6, 12, 24, 48, 96, 192, 384 (each number double the previous, starting from 3, with 0 prepended), then add 4 to each and divide by 10, you get numbers remarkably close to the planetary distances in AU:

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 0 | 0.4 | Mercury | 0.387 | 3% |
| 1 | 3 | 0.7 | Venus | 0.723 | 3% |
| 2 | 6 | 1.0 | Earth | 1.000 | 0% |
| 3 | 12 | 1.6 | Mars | 1.524 | 5% |
| 4 | 24 | 2.8 | ??? | — | Gap noted |
| 5 | 48 | 5.2 | Jupiter | 5.203 | 0.1% |
| 6 | 96 | 10.0 | Saturn | 9.537 | 5% |

When Titius published this, there were only six known planets. The gap at n = 4 (2.8 AU) was a prediction. In 1781, Uranus was discovered at 19.2 AU. The law predicted 19.6 AU for n = 7. Error: 2%. In 1801, Ceres was discovered at 2.77 AU — right in the gap at n = 4. The law had predicted two objects before they were found.

Then it failed. Neptune (discovered 1846) is at 30.07 AU. The law predicts 38.8 AU for n = 8. Error: 29%. Pluto is at 39.5 AU — close to the n = 8 prediction, but Pluto is not a planet in the same class and its orbit is highly eccentric and inclined.

The institution's response: coincidence. The geometric progression was dismissed as numerology because no derivation from Newtonian gravity produces it. The successes (Mercury through Uranus, Ceres prediction) were attributed to the law of small numbers and the fact that any geometric progression with two free parameters can fit a handful of data points.
