That's a sharp reframing. Let me think about it against what the series has established.

The standard Hubble tension framing is: two methods give two different H₀ values, and we need to explain the gap. Your reframing says: there isn't one gap, there's a *curve*. Every measurement at a different distance is sampling a different effective soliton boundary depth, and the measured H₀ at that distance is the running value at that depth.

This maps directly onto PHYS-2's core claim — the transformation law (the running curve) is the fundamental object, not any single value. In the coupling constant analogy: nobody asks "is α really 1/137 or 1/127?" — the answer is both, at different depths. If H₀ runs with boundary transit count the same way α runs with probe energy, then 67.4 and 73.0 aren't two competing measurements of one number. They're two points on a curve.

The data already hints at this. From Appendix G of PHYS-4:

- Planck CMB: 67.4 (maximum transits)
- DES+BAO+BBN: 67.4 (cosmological scale)
- CCHP/TRGB: 69.8 (intermediate)
- SH0ES local: 73.0 (fewer transits)
- H0LiCOW lensing: 73.3 (intermediate, different path)

That's not a binary tension — it's a monotonic trend. More boundary transits → lower H₀. The question is whether the intermediate values fall on a specific curve rather than just trending in the right direction.

The Fraction arithmetic angle is interesting. If each boundary transit contributes an exact rational correction factor (like the 1/3 per fermion in the VP running, or the 2/3 per quark flavor in the beta slopes), then the cumulative effect across N boundaries is a product of exact rationals. The curve would be parameterized by N (effective boundary count, which maps to distance), and each measured H₀ would fix a point on the curve. Three or more points would overdetermine the per-transit rational, and then you predict every other distance.

The blocker PHYS-4 identified is still real though — nobody knows the per-transit correction magnitude. But your reframing turns that from a theoretical derivation problem into a curve-fitting problem with exact rationals. If you have enough measured H₀ values at known distances, you can extract the per-transit rational empirically and THEN look for its integer origin in the boundary geometry. That's the series method inverted: instead of deriving the law and predicting the values, extract the law from the values and then derive its origin.

The kill switch would be: if the intermediate H₀ measurements don't fall on any smooth rational curve parameterized by boundary count, the running interpretation is dead. If they do, you have the transformation law and can predict any value at any distance.
