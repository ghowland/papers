# TIME AS TICK COUNT vs READING DEPTH AS SPATIAL SCAN

## Separating Two Concepts That Were Incorrectly Conflated

---

### I. THE CONFLATION

In the PHYS-42 perspective piece, I wrote "Probability that reading depth is physically distinct from time: 5%." This was wrong — not because the probability was wrong, but because the sentence treats reading depth and time as two versions of the same thing, where one might reduce to the other. They are not two versions of the same thing. They are two different things that happen to produce the same dilation formula in GR.

The conflation arises because GR uses one coordinate, t, and one dilation factor, √(1 − 2Φ/c²), and the standard interpretation calls this "time running slower in a gravitational field." The reading depth interpretation also uses one dilation factor, the same formula, and says "the reading changes with depth." So it looks like reading depth is an alternative name for time dilation, and the question becomes "which name is correct?"

But that framing is wrong. Time and reading depth are not competing names for the same phenomenon. They are two separate phenomena that both contribute to what we measure as "dilation."

---

### II. WHAT TIME ACTUALLY IS IN THIS FRAMEWORK

Time, in the RUM framework, is the monotonic accumulation of Planck ticks since the beginning of the universe.

That is it. Time is a count. The universe has executed N Planck ticks so far. N is a very large integer — roughly 10^61. Each tick is t_P ≈ 5.391 × 10⁻⁴⁴ seconds. The total count N × t_P ≈ 4.35 × 10¹⁷ seconds ≈ 13.8 billion years.

This count may be:

**Option A: Universal.** There is one counter. Every point in the universe is at the same tick number N. The counter advances everywhere simultaneously. What differs across locations is not the count but the local reading — how much of the count is accessible, how much is allocated to depth vs spatial displacement. In this option, "time passes slower near a mass" means "fewer of the universal ticks are readable as temporal updates at that depth." The ticks still happen. They are just allocated differently.

**Option B: Local.** There are many counters. Each point (or each boundary, or each soliton) maintains its own count. The counts are approximately synchronized but not exactly. They diverge because the local tick rate depends on the local boundary structure. In this option, "time passes slower near a mass" means "the local counter literally ticks fewer times per unit of some external reference." There is no universal tick count. There are only local counts, and they differ.

**Option C: Something in between.** There is a universal tick, but it has geometric consequences — each tick expands or deforms the boundary structure slightly. The galactic toroid gets marginally larger. The universal sphere inflates by one Planck length (or some fraction thereof). These geometric changes mean that the "same" tick produces different effects at different locations. The tick is universal but its consequences are local.

We do not know which option is correct. The experiments described in PHYS-41 (nuclear vs optical clock, NANOGrav pulsar timing, Voyager Doppler) will constrain the options, but they have not been performed yet. This is explicitly bottom-up: the data tells us which option, not the theory.

What all three options share: time is a count. It is discrete. The existence of a minimum time interval (t_P) means the universe is more like a counting machine than a continuous process. This does not mean the universe is literally a computer, or that the count is stored somewhere, or that we can read the counter. It means that the fundamental process has a minimum step size, and between steps, nothing happens. The universe advances in integer increments of t_P, not along a continuous real number line.

The evidence for discreteness is circumstantial but consistent. The Planck time exists as a natural unit. The Planck length exists as a natural unit. Their ratio is c, exactly. If time were continuous, there would be no natural minimum. The existence of a natural minimum suggests a counting process.

The geometric effect of each tick — the idea that boundaries drift, expand, or deform with each Planck step — is speculative but has observable consequences. If the universal sphere expands by one Planck length per tick, the expansion rate is l_P / t_P = c. The universe expands at the speed of light at its outermost boundary. This is consistent with the standard cosmological picture (the particle horizon expands at c), but arrives at it from a different direction: not as a speed limit on information propagation, but as a geometric consequence of the tick process.

### III. WHAT READING DEPTH ACTUALLY IS

Reading depth is completely different. It is not temporal. It is spatial. It is not a process. It is a structure.

Consider the nested soliton hierarchy: the universe contains galaxies, which contain stellar systems, which contain planets, which contain atoms, which contain nucleons, which contain quarks. Each level is a boundary. Each boundary has transformation laws. The values of physical constants — coupling strengths, masses, potentials — change as you cross boundaries. The position within this hierarchy is the reading depth.

The critical property of reading depth: it can be computed in frozen time.

If you stopped the universe at one Planck tick — froze the counter at some particular N — and scanned inward from the outermost boundary through the galaxy, through the stellar system, through the planet, through the atom, through the nucleon, to the quark — you would observe different readings at each level. The gravitational potential changes. The coupling strengths change (they run with energy scale, and energy scale maps to position in the hierarchy). The metric changes. All of these changes are functions of position in the hierarchy, not functions of time.

You could, in principle, predict every reading at every depth from a complete description of the boundary structure plus the transformation laws. No time evolution required. No ticking required. The readings are properties of the spatial structure, not properties of a temporal process.

This is the key distinction: time is a process (counting), reading depth is a structure (position).

GR conflates them because GR measures dilation — the ratio of clock rates at two different positions. This ratio depends on the gravitational potential, which is a reading depth quantity (position in the hierarchy). But GR interprets the ratio as "time runs slower," which is a temporal statement. The RUM framework says: the ratio exists because readings differ at different depths. Time (the tick count) may or may not differ. The reading definitely differs.

### IV. WHY THE DILATION FORMULA CAPTURES BOTH

The GR dilation formula is:

dτ/dt = √(1 − 2Φ/c²)

where τ is proper time (local clock reading) and t is coordinate time (distant observer's clock reading). This formula appears to be about time. It has t in it. It describes how clocks behave.

But decompose what it actually measures.

A clock is a device that counts cycles of some periodic process. An atomic clock counts oscillations of a cesium hyperfine transition. A GPS satellite clock counts oscillations of a rubidium frequency standard. A pulsar "clock" counts rotations of a neutron star.

The oscillation frequency of any of these clocks depends on the local values of the physical constants — the fine structure constant, the nuclear magneton, the electron mass. These are readings. They are properties of the position in the hierarchy.

When you place a clock at a different gravitational potential, you are placing it at a different reading depth. The physical constants have (infinitesimally) different values. The oscillation frequency changes. The clock ticks at a different rate. You measure this as "time dilation."

But what actually changed? Two things, potentially:

1. The reading depth changed — the local values of constants shifted due to the different position in the hierarchy. This affects the oscillation frequency through the physics of the clock.

2. The tick count may or may not have changed — depending on whether time is universal (Option A), local (Option B), or geometric (Option C).

The dilation formula captures the net effect. It does not distinguish the two contributions. If reading depth alone causes the dilation (the tick count is universal but the readings differ), you get the same formula. If the tick count alone causes the dilation (readings are the same everywhere but clocks tick at different local rates), you get the same formula. If both contribute, you still get the same formula. The formula is degenerate with respect to the mechanism.

This is why the PHYS-42 experiment cannot distinguish reading depth from time. Every GR test measures the combined effect. Decomposing it into "reading depth contribution" and "tick count contribution" requires a measurement that is sensitive to one but not the other.

### V. THE NUCLEAR CLOCK TEST, REVISITED

The thorium-229 nuclear clock test, from PHYS-41, is now better motivated by this decomposition.

A strontium optical clock measures oscillations of an electronic transition. The transition frequency depends on α (fine structure constant), m_e (electron mass), and the strontium nuclear charge. All of these are electromagnetic readings — they probe the electromagnetic sector of the soliton hierarchy.

A thorium-229 nuclear clock measures oscillations of a nuclear isomer transition. The transition frequency depends on the strong nuclear force, nuclear structure, and the specific arrangement of nucleons in the thorium-229 nucleus. These are strong-force readings — they probe a different sector of the hierarchy.

In standard GR, both clocks experience the same dilation because the metric couples universally to all sectors. The gravitational redshift is the same for electromagnetic oscillations and nuclear oscillations. This is the equivalence principle.

In the reading depth interpretation, the two clocks probe different depths in the hierarchy. The electromagnetic reading depth (accessible to the strontium clock) and the strong-force reading depth (accessible to the thorium clock) are both functions of position in the gravitational hierarchy, but they need not change at exactly the same rate across a boundary. If the transformation law across a gravitational boundary has sector-dependent terms — if the reading shifts by slightly different amounts for electromagnetic vs strong-force observables — the two clocks will disagree.

This disagreement would be invisible to any single-sector measurement. Every GR test in the PHYS-42 experiment uses electromagnetic observables (photon frequencies, atomic clocks, electromagnetic radiation from pulsars). They all probe the same sector. They all agree with each other and with GR. The nuclear clock introduces a second sector. Agreement between the two sectors confirms the equivalence principle (and makes reading depth indistinguishable from standard GR at that scale). Disagreement violates the equivalence principle and provides direct evidence for sector-dependent reading depth.

Note what this test probes: it probes whether reading depth is the same across sectors at a given gravitational potential. It does not probe whether the tick count is universal or local. The tick count question requires a different kind of test — one that compares spatially separated clocks without a gravitational potential difference, looking for intrinsic rate differences that would indicate local vs universal counting.

### VI. THE COUNTING MACHINE INTERPRETATION

The statement "the universe seems more like a counting machine than a continuum number line process" deserves careful unpacking.

A continuum model says: time is a real number. Between any two moments, there are uncountably many intermediate moments. The evolution from t₁ to t₂ passes through every real number in the interval [t₁, t₂]. Physics is described by differential equations, which assume continuous variation.

A counting model says: time is an integer. Between tick N and tick N+1, there is nothing. The universe is in state S(N), then it is in state S(N+1). The transition S(N) → S(N+1) is not decomposable into sub-steps. Physics is described by difference equations on integers, which approximate differential equations when N is large.

The evidence does not definitively favor either model. The success of differential equations in physics is extraordinary — GR, QFT, fluid dynamics, everything uses continuous mathematics. But differential equations also work extremely well as approximations to difference equations when the step size is small. And t_P = 5.39 × 10⁻⁴⁴ seconds is extraordinarily small. No experiment has ever probed time intervals shorter than about 10⁻²⁵ seconds (high-energy particle collisions). The gap between 10⁻²⁵ and 10⁻⁴⁴ is 19 orders of magnitude. We have no direct evidence about what happens at the Planck scale.

What the counting interpretation buys you:

1. **Discreteness explains the Planck scale naturally.** If time is an integer count of t_P ticks, there is no mystery about why t_P exists as a minimum — it is the step size. The Planck length l_P is the spatial resolution per tick. The speed of light c = l_P/t_P is the ratio of spatial resolution to temporal resolution, which is the maximum rate at which spatial readings can change. No dynamics needed. No relativistic postulates needed. Just the resolution of the counting process.

2. **The arrow of time is trivial.** If time is a count, it increases monotonically. N+1 > N. The arrow of time is not a thermodynamic mystery requiring special boundary conditions or low-entropy initial states. It is a property of counting. You cannot count backwards. The count always increases.

3. **The measurement problem in quantum mechanics may be a counting artifact.** If the universe advances in discrete steps, and each step involves a definite state transition S(N) → S(N+1), then the "collapse" of the wave function is not a mysterious non-unitary process — it is just what happens at each tick. Between ticks, the state is not defined (or is defined only as a computational intermediate that is never physically realized). This is speculative and far beyond what the current framework can support, but it is a natural consequence of the counting interpretation.

What the counting interpretation costs you:

1. **Lorentz invariance becomes approximate.** A discrete spacetime lattice generically breaks Lorentz symmetry. The Planck-scale discreteness would produce detectable effects in the dispersion of high-energy photons from distant gamma-ray bursts — photons of different energies would arrive at slightly different times. Current limits from Fermi-LAT observations (GRB 090510) constrain the energy scale of Lorentz violation to above the Planck scale (Abdo et al. 2009). This is exactly where the counting model predicts it. The constraint is therefore consistent with, but does not confirm, the counting model.

2. **Continuous symmetries become discrete.** Rotational invariance, translational invariance, and boost invariance are all continuous symmetries that are broken by any lattice. The counting model requires that these symmetries emerge in the large-N limit (which they do, in the same way that a crystal lattice appears continuous at macroscopic scales) and that the deviations are below current measurement precision (which they are, given the 19-order gap between probed and Planck scales).

### VII. THE GEOMETRIC EFFECT OF TICKS

The speculation that "each tick has a geometric effect on the boundaries" connects the counting process to the reading depth structure.

If each Planck tick expands the outermost boundary by one Planck length (or some related increment), then:

- The universe has expanded by N × l_P since the first tick. With N ~ 10⁶¹ and l_P ~ 10⁻³⁵ m, this gives a current size of ~10²⁶ m, which is the right order of magnitude for the observable universe (actual: 4.4 × 10²⁶ m to the particle horizon).

- The expansion rate is l_P/t_P = c, consistent with the cosmic horizon expanding at c.

- Deeper boundaries (galaxies, stars, planets) are affected by the expansion differently depending on their binding energy. Gravitationally bound systems do not expand with the universe because their internal binding exceeds the expansion increment per tick. Unbound regions (intergalactic space) expand because nothing resists the per-tick increment.

- The "drift" of numbers over time — the idea that physical constants might change slowly across cosmic history — would be a consequence of the accumulated geometric effect of 10⁶¹ ticks on the boundary structure. Coupling constants measured at different cosmological epochs (via quasar absorption spectra) would differ by amounts proportional to ΔN/N, where ΔN is the difference in tick counts between epochs.

This is where the concept bites: the tick count changes the reading depth structure. Time (the count) modifies space (the hierarchy). The two are not independent. They are coupled through the geometric effect of each tick on the boundaries.

This coupling is exactly what GR describes — spacetime, not space and time separately. The metric tensor encodes both spatial curvature and temporal dilation in a single geometric object. The counting-plus-reading-depth decomposition is not an alternative to GR. It is a decomposition of what GR describes as a unified spacetime geometry into two components: a discrete temporal process (counting) and a spatial structure (reading depth). The GR metric is the combined effect of both.

### VIII. WHAT THE EXPERIMENTS WILL TELL US

The decomposition into tick count vs reading depth generates specific experimental questions that are distinct from the questions PHYS-42 already answered.

**Question 1: Is the tick universal or local?**

Test: Compare two clocks at the same gravitational potential, separated by a large distance (e.g., one at the galactic center and one at the galactic edge, both at the same height above the galactic plane). Standard GR predicts zero difference (same potential = same dilation). If the tick is local and the local tick rates differ due to the different boundary environment (closer to the galactic center vs farther out), there would be a residual difference that GR does not predict.

Current constraint: pulsar timing arrays (NANOGrav, EPTA, PPTA) measure pulse arrival times from pulsars at various galactocentric radii. Any systematic trend of pulsar spin-down rates with galactocentric radius, beyond what is explained by the galactic potential gradient, would suggest local tick variation. Current data shows no such trend, but the sensitivity is limited by pulsar spin-down noise and proper motion uncertainties.

**Question 2: Is reading depth sector-dependent?**

Test: the thorium-229 nuclear clock vs strontium optical clock, as described above. If the electromagnetic and strong-force readings change at different rates across a gravitational boundary, the clocks disagree. This probes reading depth structure, not tick count.

Timeline: 3-5 years for clock-quality thorium-229 interrogation.

**Question 3: Does the tick have a geometric effect?**

Test: measure whether the fine structure constant α changes over cosmological timescales. The accumulated geometric effect of ticks on boundaries would produce a drift in α proportional to the cosmic time elapsed. Current constraints from quasar absorption spectra (Webb et al., Murphy et al.) are at the level of Δα/α < 10⁻⁶ over 10¹⁰ years. This corresponds to a per-tick drift of < 10⁻⁶/10⁶¹ = 10⁻⁶⁷ per tick, which is consistent with either zero drift or a drift too small to detect.

A stronger test: compare atomic clock rates at different epochs using the same technology. The Oklo natural reactor (2 billion years ago) constrains Δα/α < 10⁻⁷ over 2 × 10⁹ years. Future space missions with optical clocks could push this to 10⁻⁸ or better.

**Question 4: Is spacetime discrete at the Planck scale?**

Test: gamma-ray burst photon dispersion. If spacetime is a discrete lattice with spacing l_P, photons of different energies traverse the lattice at slightly different speeds, producing energy-dependent arrival times. The dispersion is proportional to E/E_Planck per unit distance. For a GRB at cosmological distances (z ~ 1, distance ~ 10²⁶ m), the arrival time difference between a 10 GeV photon and a 100 MeV photon would be of order (10 GeV / 10¹⁹ GeV) × 10²⁶ m / c ~ 10⁻¹² seconds, which is at the edge of current detection capability.

Current constraint: Fermi-LAT has constrained the Lorentz violation scale to > 1.2 E_Planck (Vasileiou et al. 2013). This means either spacetime is not discrete, or the discreteness does not produce first-order dispersion, or the specific form of the discreteness is different from a simple lattice.

### IX. THE TWO CONCEPTS, CLEANLY SEPARATED

To summarize for a new researcher:

**Time (tick count):**
- Monotonic integer process
- May be universal or local
- Has potential geometric consequences (boundary drift, expansion)
- The arrow of time is a property of counting (N+1 > N)
- The Planck time is the step size
- Cannot be "scanned" — it only goes forward
- May be the same everywhere but experienced differently (due to boundary structure)
- May be literally different everywhere (local counters)
- Experiments needed: pulsar timing vs galactocentric radius, α drift over cosmic time, GRB dispersion

**Reading depth (spatial structure):**
- Position within the nested soliton hierarchy
- Computable in frozen time — no evolution required
- Determines local values of physical constants
- Determines gravitational dilation through Φ/c²
- Can be scanned mathematically: given the integers and transformation laws, predict the value at any depth
- May be sector-dependent (electromagnetic vs strong-force readings differ at same depth)
- Experiments needed: nuclear clock vs optical clock, boundary anomalies (Voyager, G scatter)

**What GR measures:**
- The combined effect of both: dτ/dt = √(1 − 2Φ/c²)
- This formula is degenerate — it cannot decompose the dilation into tick-count vs reading-depth contributions
- Every GR test in PHYS-42 confirms the combined formula
- No GR test in PHYS-42 distinguishes the two components

**The question is not "is reading depth physically distinct from time?"**

The question is: "what fraction of the observed dilation comes from reading depth (spatial structure) and what fraction comes from tick count variation (temporal process)?" The answer might be 100% reading depth and 0% tick count (the tick is universal, only readings change). Or 100% tick count and 0% reading depth (the tick is local, readings are the same everywhere). Or some combination. The GR formula gives the sum. The decomposition requires experiments that are sensitive to one component but not the other.

This is what the next phase of the project should target. Not more GR confirmations (those are done). Not more integer-chain computations (those are independent of this question). But specifically designed experiments — or specifically designed derivations within the existing framework — that make different predictions depending on whether the dilation is structural (reading depth) or processual (tick count).

The soliton framework has the right vocabulary for this decomposition. The boundaries are spatial structure (reading depth). The tick process advances the boundaries geometrically (time). The two are coupled but distinct. The experiments of the next decade — nuclear clocks, pulsar timing, gravitational wave detectors — will tell us how they combine. It is not top-down theory. It is bottom-up measurement. The framework provides the language. Nature provides the answer.
