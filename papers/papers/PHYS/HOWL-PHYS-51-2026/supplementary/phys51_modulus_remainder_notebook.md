### 1. THE CONCEPT

Unpacking this in framework language:

But the modulus operates at a specific coupling, at a specific scale, at a specific level in the hierarchy. Different solitons (electron, proton, atom, nucleus) have different boundary couplings, different effective moduli at their specific scales. **Remainder = the tension that accumulates.** The remainder is what's left after the modulus does its geometric work. The concept says: this growing complexity IS the tension. The remainder accumulates non-spherical content until it forces a transition — a "shell jump" — where the system reorganizes into a new soliton configuration.

### 2. WHAT IS A SHELL JUMP?

In atomic physics, a shell jump is an electron transitioning from one energy level to another. The electron absorbs or emits a photon and moves to a different orbital. The "tension" is the energy mismatch between the electron's current state and an available state. The "modulus" is the Rydberg constant (which contains β through R_∞ = α²m_e c/2) — it sets the spacing between shells.

In the soliton framework, a "shell jump" would be a transition between soliton levels in the hierarchy. Not just electron orbitals, but transitions between different TYPES of solitons: electron → muon → tau (lepton shells), quark → hadron → nucleus (confinement shells), atom → molecule → bulk (binding shells).

Each level in the hierarchy has its own modulus (its own effective β at its own scale) and its own remainder (the accumulated tension from everything the modulus didn't account for). When the remainder exceeds a threshold — when the tension becomes too large for the current soliton shell to contain — the system jumps to the next shell.

### 5. THE TWO TYPES OF REMAINDER AS TWO TYPES OF TENSION

The remainder has two layers. In the tension reading, they are two different kinds of tension:

**Layer 1 (number-theoretic β⁰): algebraic tension.** The rational numbers, ζ values, and polylogarithms represent RADIAL content — momentum magnitudes, diagram counts, nesting depths. This tension is about how many diagrams contribute, how deeply nested the virtual processes are, how the combinatorics of quantum field theory grow with order. It's algebraic, countable, and in principle manageable. The cancellation with the modulus is between this algebraic tension and the angular geometry. They nearly cancel because they measure different aspects of the SAME spherical integral — the radial and angular parts of a spherical momentum integration.

**Layer 2 (toroidal-geometric β⁰): topological tension.** The elliptic periods K(k) and E(k) represent ANGULAR content on a different manifold — the torus, not the sphere. This tension is about the topology of the virtual process. At four loops, the Feynman diagrams include topologies (81 and 83 in Laporta's classification) that have toroidal momentum circulation. The tension is not "too many diagrams" (that's algebraic) but "wrong geometry" (that's topological). The spherical framework cannot absorb this content. It's a fundamentally new kind of tension.

The shell jump at loop 4 is driven by TOPOLOGICAL tension, not algebraic tension. Algebraic tension (layer 1) can be managed by tighter cancellation with the modulus — 90%, 99.5%, eventually 99.99%. Topological tension (layer 2) cannot be cancelled at all because it's in a different geometric basis.

This distinction — algebraic vs topological tension — might apply at every level of the soliton hierarchy:

- **Atomic shell jumps:** driven by algebraic tension (electron energy exceeds orbital capacity). The modulus (Rydberg, which involves β through α) sets the spacing. The transition is algebraic — same geometry (Coulomb), different quantum number.

- **Particle decay:** driven by topological tension? The muon decays because its internal geometry (toroidal at (m_μ/m_e)² amplification) exceeds the stability boundary of the lepton soliton. The transition is topological — different geometry (muon → electron involves a change in the mass/coupling configuration).

- **Confinement/deconfinement:** driven by topological tension. The QCD phase transition at high temperature is literally a change in topology — deconfined quarks (perturbative, spherical geometry) vs confined hadrons (non-perturbative, possibly toroidal flux-tube geometry).
