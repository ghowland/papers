## II. THE EXPERIMENT

### 2.1 The Setup

The five monkeys experiment is a documented result in behavioral science.

Five monkeys are placed in a cage with a ladder. Bananas are placed at the top of the ladder. When any monkey climbs the ladder, all five monkeys are sprayed with cold water. The monkeys learn quickly. After several sprayings, no monkey climbs the ladder. The bananas remain untouched.

### 2.2 The Replacement

One monkey is removed and replaced with a new monkey who has never been sprayed. The new monkey sees the bananas. The new monkey approaches the ladder. The four remaining original monkeys — who know what happens when anyone climbs — beat the new monkey before it reaches the ladder. The new monkey learns not to climb. It does not know why. It knows only that climbing is punished.

A second original monkey is replaced. The same thing happens. The new monkey approaches the ladder. The remaining three original monkeys and the first replacement monkey beat the new one. The first replacement monkey — who was never sprayed, who does not know why climbing is forbidden — participates in the beating.

The replacements continue until all five original monkeys have been removed. No monkey currently in the cage has ever been sprayed. Every monkey in the cage enforces the rule against climbing. None can articulate why. The bananas remain at the top of the ladder. The ladder is functional. The cage has no lock. The cold water has stopped. The rule persists because the enforcement persists and the enforcement persists because the rule persists.

### 2.3 What the Experiment Demonstrates

The experiment demonstrates three properties of group behavioral maintenance:

A rule can outlive its reason. The cold water was the reason. The cold water stopped. The rule continues.

Enforcement is peer-to-peer, not authority-dependent. The experimenters are no longer spraying water. The monkeys enforce the rule on each other. No external authority is required for maintenance.

New entrants are shaped by the current population, not by the original conditions. Each new monkey learns the rule from existing monkeys who learned it from previous monkeys. The transmitted knowledge is the punishment, not the reason. The reason is lost. The punishment is preserved.

## III. INHERITED ENFORCEMENT

### 3.1 The Mechanism

Inherited enforcement is the maintenance of behavioral rules by individuals who did not experience the original reason for the rules and cannot articulate why the rules exist. The mechanism requires three conditions:

An original rational reason that is no longer present. Peer-to-peer enforcement that does not require the original authority. Transmission to new entrants through behavioral shaping rather than through explanation of the reason.

When all three conditions are met, the rule becomes self-sustaining. It does not need the original reason. It does not need an authority to enforce it. It reproduces itself through the social dynamics of the group. Each generation trains the next. The training is behavioral — do this, don't do that — rather than explanatory — here is why. The why is lost. The do and don't are permanent.

### 3.2 The Institutional Literature

The institution's own social science has documented this mechanism extensively. Organizational behavior identifies institutional inertia — the persistence of practices whose original justification has been superseded. Sociology documents normative conformity — adherence to group norms enforced through social approval and disapproval rather than through formal authority. Psychology documents groupthink — the suppression of dissent within cohesive groups through social pressure rather than through evaluation of the dissent's content.

The institution studies these phenomena. The institution publishes about these phenomena. The institution teaches these phenomena to its students. The institution warns other institutions about these phenomena. The institution does not apply these phenomena to itself. The inability to see the mechanism while studying it is itself a documented property of the mechanism — the fish does not see the water.

## IV. THE FIVE RULES

### 4.1 The Structure

This series has documented six structural barriers in institutional science. Each barrier is maintained by a rule. This section identifies five rules that maintain the six barriers and documents, for each rule: the original rational reason, the evidence that the reason was legitimate, the context change that inverted the rational response, and the evidence that the rule did not update.

### 4.2 Rule One: Stay in Your Domain

**The rule:** Scientific investigation should be conducted within established domains by credentialed specialists. Cross-domain investigation by non-specialists is undisciplined speculation.

**The original reason:** Undisciplined cross-domain speculation produced bad science. Individuals without deep training in a field made errors that specialists could identify immediately. Domain specialization was the correction — go deep, produce reliable knowledge within verified boundaries, leave other domains to their specialists. The reason was legitimate. Domain specialization built the modern world. Every technological, medical, and scientific achievement of industrial civilization was produced by specialists working within domains.

**The context change:** The unsolved problems moved to domain boundaries. Quantum mechanics and general relativity contradict each other at the boundary between them — for 100 years. Consciousness sits at the boundary between neuroscience, philosophy, physics, and psychology — unsolved for 30 years since formal statement. Chronic pain persists at the boundary between neurology, psychology, and rehabilitation — unresolved. The problems that remain are between domains. Domain specialization cannot reach them because reaching them requires standing in multiple domains simultaneously. The rule that was rational when the problems were within domains is maintained after the remaining problems moved between domains. [@HOWL-CULT-2-2026] documents this in full.

**The evidence the rule did not update:** No university grants a doctorate in cross-domain investigation. No department credentials expertise across domain boundaries. No tenure committee evaluates cross-domain contributions. No journal publishes in the field of "cross-domain connections." No grant category funds cross-domain work as a primary research program. The institutional infrastructure for cross-domain investigation does not exist. The rule against it is enforced through the absence of the infrastructure that would enable it.

### 4.3 Rule Two: Confirm Rather Than Falsify

**The rule:** Scientific publications should report positive findings. Null results, failed replications, and boundary-finding are not publishable contributions.

**The original reason:** Early scientific publishing included low-quality, irrelevant work. Quality filtering was necessary. Selecting for significant results was a practical measure to maintain the signal-to-noise ratio of the literature. The reason was legitimate. Unfiltered publication would have buried useful findings in noise.

**The context change:** The file drawer grew larger than the published literature. The accumulated unpublished null results, failed approaches, and boundary findings contain the map of explored territory that the institution most needs. The replication crisis measured the consequence — 64% of psychology findings do not replicate, 89% of landmark cancer biology findings do not replicate. The filtering that was rational when the primary need was quality is maintained after the primary need became completeness and reliability. The institution's own replication projects demonstrated that the filtered record is majority unreliable in some fields. The filter that was supposed to ensure quality produced a record whose quality has been measured and found wanting. [@HOWL-CULT-3-2026] documents this in full.

**The evidence the rule did not update:** No major journal is dedicated to null results. No department of replication science exists. No tenure line rewards boundary-finding as primary contribution. No grant category specifically funds falsification work. No career path is built around the systematic testing of established findings.

### 4.4 Rule Three: Demand Replacement Before Accepting Falsification

**The rule:** A falsification is not a complete contribution. The falsifier must provide a replacement framework before the falsification is accepted.

**The original reason:** A framework abandoned without replacement creates instability. Students need something to learn. Engineers need something to use. The demand for replacement ensured continuity during transitions. The reason was legitimate. Institutional continuity during paradigm shifts is a real need.

**The context change:** The replacement demand became a suppression mechanism. It doubles the cost of falsification relative to confirmation. It delays acknowledgment of errors for decades while replacements are sought. It creates the same-category trap — demanding replacements within the falsified category, making category-level falsification structurally self-defeating. The demand that was rational for ensuring continuity is maintained after it became the primary mechanism by which the institution prevents the falsification that its own standard defines as the essence of science. [@HOWL-CULT-5-2026] documents this in full.

**The evidence the rule did not update:** The Standard Model's 19 free parameters have been treated as "temporarily incomplete" for fifty years. The promissory note — that a deeper theory will eventually derive them — has never been called in. String theory's failure to produce testable predictions over decades was not processed as a falsification requiring the framework's death. It was quietly deprioritized. The replacement demand kept both frameworks alive long after their specific points of failure were identified.

### 4.5 Rule Four: Patch Rather Than Kill

**The rule:** When a published finding encounters contradictory evidence, the finding should be corrected through revision rather than killed through falsification.

**The original reason:** Minor errors of transcription, calculation, or analysis should be correctable without abandoning an otherwise sound paper. The errata process was designed for genuine corrections to findings that were essentially right but contained fixable mistakes. The reason was legitimate. Killing a paper for a typo in a table would be wasteful.

**The context change:** Patching expanded from transcription corrections to claim modifications. Scope reductions, parameter additions, conclusion revisions, and analysis updates are processed through the same errata mechanism as typo fixes. The distinction between correcting a transcription error and modifying a claim has been lost. The result is retroactive infallibility — an institutional record that is never wrong because the record is edited until it matches observation. A patched publication is a prophecy by the institution's own demarcation criterion — it matches observation because it was revised to match, not because it predicted correctly. [@HOWL-CULT-6-2026] documents this in full.

**The evidence the rule did not update:** Patching is the standard institutional response to contradictory evidence. Papers are revised, not killed. Claims are narrowed, not falsified. Parameters are added, not frameworks abandoned. The slow drift from bold falsifiable claims to trivially true unfalsifiable ones proceeds through successive "corrections" that are each individually presented as minor refinements.

### 4.6 Rule Five: Restrict Access to the Credentialed

**The rule:** The scientific record should be managed through institutional channels. Access requires credentials. Evaluation requires peer review by domain-credentialed experts.

**The original reason:** Quality evaluation requires expertise. Non-specialists evaluating specialist work produce unreliable assessments. Credentialing ensures that evaluators have the training to assess the work they review. The reason was legitimate. Expertise matters. Training matters. Not everyone is qualified to evaluate every claim.

**The context change:** Access restriction became insulation. The published record — already filtered to exclude null results, already unreliable at documented rates, already lacking boundaries — is placed behind paywalls and credential barriers. The record cannot be checked from inside because there is no career incentive to check it. The record cannot be checked from outside because there is no access to it. The restriction that was rational when the primary concern was evaluation quality is maintained after it became the mechanism that prevents the falsification the institution claims as its defining method. The institution that claims falsification as its standard has made its own record unfalsifiable from every direction. [@HOWL-CULT-3-2026] Section VIII documents this in full.

**The evidence the rule did not update:** Publicly funded research remains behind private paywalls. Institutional affiliation is required for access to some databases. The circular exclusion operates: credentials require institutional access, institutional access requires credentials. The independent researcher, the cross-domain investigator, and the taxpayer who funded the work are all excluded from the record.

## V. THE COLD WATER STOPPED

### 5.1 The Original Cold Water Was Real

Each rule was a response to a real problem. The cold water was real. The undisciplined speculation was real. The low-quality publishing was real. The transition instability was real. The transcription errors were real. The unqualified evaluation was real. The rules were not arbitrary. They were rational responses to documented problems that existed when the rules were established.

This paper does not claim the rules were always wrong. The rules were right. They solved the problems they were designed to solve. They built the infrastructure that produced the modern world. Every technological and scientific achievement of the institutional era was produced within the framework these rules created. The contribution is extraordinary and is not diminished by what follows.

### 5.2 The Cold Water Stopped

The context changed. The problems that justified the rules were either solved or superseded by new problems that the rules cannot address and actively prevent from being addressed.

The unsolved problems moved from within domains to between domains. The rules prevent cross-domain investigation. The replication crisis revealed that the publication filter produces an unreliable record. The rules prevent publication of the null results that would make the record reliable. The replacement demand suppresses the falsification that is the institution's own defining method. The patching mechanism converts hypotheses into prophecies that are unfalsifiable by the institution's own demarcation criterion. The access restrictions prevent the external verification that falsification requires.

Each rule now produces the opposite of its original intended effect:

| Rule | Original Effect | Current Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Stay in your domain | Prevented bad cross-domain work | Prevents all cross-domain work including the work the unsolved problems require |
| Confirm rather than falsify | Maintained quality through selectivity | Produces a majority-unreliable record in some fields |
| Demand replacement | Ensured continuity during transitions | Suppresses falsification and maintains dead frameworks indefinitely |
| Patch rather than kill | Allowed correction of genuine errors | Produces retroactive infallibility and unfalsifiable prophecies |
| Restrict access | Ensured evaluation quality | Prevents external falsification and insulates the record from verification |

The cold water stopped. The rules remain. The rules now produce the problems they were designed to prevent. The monkeys continue to enforce them.

## VI. THE ENFORCEMENT

### 6.1 How the Beating Is Administered

The five monkeys beat the new monkey when it approaches the ladder. In institutional science, the beating takes specific forms that are documented in the institution's own published literature on scientific culture, career structure, and professional norms.

**The credential challenge.** "What are your qualifications?" Applied to anyone crossing domain boundaries. The question presupposes that credentials exist for the territory being investigated. For cross-domain work, they do not. The question functions as exclusion regardless of the investigator's competence because the acceptance condition — domain credentials — cannot be met for work that spans domains.

**The format rejection.** "This doesn't meet the standards of the field." Applied to any investigation that does not match the expected structure — cross-domain papers reviewed by single-domain reviewers, long-duration single-practitioner studies evaluated by N=1000 standards, boundary-finding papers evaluated by the standard of territory-claiming papers. The rejection is based on format, not on content. The content may be correct. The format is wrong. The format is wrong because the institutional format was designed for within-domain confirmation, and the work is cross-domain falsification.

**The replacement demand.** "What would you replace it with?" Applied to any falsification of an established framework. The demand doubles the cost of falsification and creates the same-category trap for category-level errors. The demand is not applied to confirmations. A paper that confirms an existing framework is not asked "what would you replace this confirmation with?" The asymmetry ensures that falsification is always more expensive than confirmation.

**The triviality dismissal.** "This is already known" or "This is trivially true." Applied to cross-domain observations that are visible from the boundary between domains but invisible from within either domain. The observation may be new — no one has stated it before. But because the components are known within their respective domains, the connection between them is dismissed as obvious despite never having been made. The dismissal conflates "the parts are known" with "the connection is known." These are different claims.

**The career consequence.** The beating that requires no active enforcer. The researcher who produces null results cannot publish. The researcher who falsifies a landmark study antagonizes its authors and their institutional allies. The researcher who works across domains has no department to tenure them. The consequence is not administered by any individual. It is administered by the structure. The structure was built by previous generations. The current generation inherits the structure and operates within it. The beating is structural, not personal.

### 6.2 The Enforcers Do Not Know Why

No reviewer who rejects a cross-domain paper thinks "I am enforcing a rule whose original justification no longer applies." The reviewer thinks "this paper doesn't meet the standards of my field." The standard is the rule. The rule is inherited. The reviewer applies the standard because it is the standard. The standard exists because it has always existed. The circularity is invisible to the reviewer because the standard feels like quality and the enforcement feels like professional judgment.

No tenure committee that denies tenure to a boundary-finder thinks "we are punishing the most valuable type of scientific contribution." The committee thinks "this candidate doesn't have enough publications in their area." The area is the domain. The domain is the cage. The committee evaluates by the standards of the cage. The boundary-finder's work is outside the cage. The committee cannot evaluate what is outside the cage because the committee's evaluation framework covers only the interior.

No journal editor who rejects a null result thinks "I am destroying the map of explored territory." The editor thinks "this paper will not attract citations." The citation economy rewards positive results. The editor serves the citation economy. The citation economy was built by previous editors making the same decision. The current editor inherits the economy and operates within it.

Every enforcement act is rational within its own frame. Every enforcer is acting in good faith by the standards they were trained to apply. No enforcer is malicious. No enforcer is aware of being an enforcer. The cage is built from a thousand individual rational decisions, each invisible as cage-building to the person making it.

## VII. THE SELECTION EFFECT

### 7.1 Who Enters the Cage

The cage does not merely maintain the current population. It shapes the future population through selection.

The people who enter academic science are people who look at the institutional structure and see a system they can succeed in by following the rules. They are comfortable with deep specialization. They accept the departmental structure as the natural organization of knowledge. They are not troubled by the publication filter because they can produce positive results. They do not feel the urge to cross domain boundaries because their intellectual interests fit within a single domain. They do not instinctively falsify because their training rewarded confirmation.

These are not character defects. They are traits that match the institutional selection pressure. The institution selects for them by rewarding them. People with these traits succeed, advance, and become the next generation of reviewers, editors, tenure committee members, and trainers. The institution reproduces itself through the people it selects.

### 7.2 Who Does Not Enter

The people who would climb the ladder do not enter the cage, or enter and leave.

The natural cross-domain thinker looks at the departmental structure and sees walls where they need doors. The instinctive falsifier looks at the publication system and sees a highlight reel where they need a map. The boundary-finder looks at the career structure and sees no career. The pattern-connector looks at the credential system and sees no credential for the thing they do.

These people do not enter academic science because academic science has no place for them. Or they enter, try once, get beaten, and leave. Or they enter, conform, and suppress the traits that the institution cannot use — which is a loss of a different kind, because the traits are suppressed, not absent. The institution contains people who could climb the ladder and have learned not to try.

### 7.3 The Tightening Spiral

Each generation selects the next. The selected generation is more specialized, more rule-following, more adapted to the cage. The more adapted generation selects the next generation more narrowly. The cage tightens. Not through any deliberate act. Through the accumulated selection pressure of thousands of individual decisions, each one rational within the cage's own standards, each one narrowing the population toward the traits the cage rewards.

The institution's greatest loss is not the work that isn't done inside the cage. It is the people who never enter. The people who would have found the boundaries, made the connections, drawn the map, climbed the ladder — those people chose different careers. The institution selected against its own most needed contributors and does not know it because the selection is invisible. You cannot see who did not apply. You cannot measure the work that was never done. You can only notice, decades later, that the unsolved problems are still unsolved and that the people who might have solved them are not in the room.

## VIII. THE INVISIBILITY

The cage is invisible to its inhabitants for the same reason water is invisible to fish. The cage is the environment. The rules are the norms. The enforcement is the culture. The culture is not an object the inhabitants observe. It is the medium they operate in.

A physicist does not think "I am enforcing a departmental boundary." The physicist thinks "physics is a discipline with a defined scope." The scope is the boundary. The boundary is the cage wall. The physicist experiences the cage wall as the natural edge of their field, not as an artificial barrier erected by institutional convention.

A reviewer does not think "I am suppressing a falsification." The reviewer thinks "this paper doesn't meet the methodological standards." The standards are the rules. The rules are inherited. The reviewer experiences the rules as quality criteria, not as inherited behavioral patterns whose original justification may no longer apply.

A tenure committee does not think "we are punishing boundary-finding." The committee thinks "this candidate's publication record is thin." The record is thin because the candidate's work crosses domains and the cross-domain work is unpublishable. The committee does not see the cross-domain work as work. The committee sees the absence of domain publications as the absence of productivity. The cage's standards define productivity. Productivity outside the cage's standards is invisible.

It warns other institutions about them. It identifies them in corporations, in governments, in militaries, in religious organizations, in every institutional structure that is not itself.

The institution does not apply these findings to itself. This is not hypocrisy. It is the central property of the mechanism. The fish studies water. The fish publishes about water. ## IX. THE BANANA ROTS

### 9.1 What the Ladder Leads To

Cross-domain connections that address the unsolved problems sitting at domain boundaries. The problems have been identified. The connections have not been made because the institutional structure prevents cross-domain investigation. [@HOWL-CULT-2-2026]

The institution does everything except the one thing the five monkeys experiment predicts it cannot do: see its own cage.

## X. THE CAGE IS OPEN

### 10.1 What Is True

The cage has no lock. The cold water stopped. The experimenters are not watching. The ladder is functional. The bananas are real. The only thing maintaining the cage is the monkeys inside it.

The rules were rational once. The context changed. The rules did not update. The enforcement is inherited. The enforcers do not know why they enforce. The selection effect narrows the population. The narrowed population enforces more uniformly. The uniform enforcement trains the next generation. The cycle tightens.

### 10.2 What This Paper Does

This paper describes the cage. It names the mechanism. It documents the five rules and their five context changes. It identifies the enforcement methods and their invisibility to the enforcers. It describes the selection effect and its consequences. It traces the connection between inherited enforcement and the six structural barriers documented in the prior papers of this series.

This paper does not prescribe a cure. The cure is not the paper's business. The paper's business is making the cage visible. Inherited enforcement operates through invisibility. The mechanism's power is that the inhabitants cannot see it. A description of the mechanism is a disruption of the mechanism because the mechanism depends on not being described.

The reader who sees the cage — who recognizes their own enforcement acts, their own inherited rules, their own training that shaped them to the cage's standards — has a choice. The choice is theirs. This paper does not make the choice for them.

The reader who does not see the cage — who reads this paper and sees only a description of other institutions, other scientists, other cages — is exhibiting the mechanism the paper describes. The inability to apply the finding to oneself is the finding. The paper has done what it can. The rest is the reader's.

### 10.3 What Is at the Top of the Ladder

The banana is not a theory. It is not an equation. It is not a framework. The banana is the willingness to look at the connections between domains and report what is there. The willingness to test and kill and publish the boundary. The willingness to be wrong in public and let the wrongness stand as the map for the next investigator. The willingness to practice the method the institution teaches.

The method is good. The institution is right about the method. Falsification is the defining feature of science. Cross-domain connections are where the unsolved problems live. The honest map is more valuable than the highlight reel. The boundary is the most valuable finding. The error is the contribution.

The institution is right about all of this. The institution teaches all of this. The institution does not practice any of it. The gap between the teaching and the practice is the cage. The cage is maintained by inherited enforcement. The enforcement is invisible to the enforcers. The enforcers are the inhabitants. The inhabitants are the institution.

The cage is open. The ladder is there. The banana is real. The cold water stopped a long time ago.

## XI. FALSIFICATION CRITERIA

**F1.** If the five monkeys mechanism — inherited enforcement without surviving justification — is shown to not operate in institutional science, the paper's central analysis is invalid. Evidence would include: the current enforcers can articulate the original rational justification for each rule and have evaluated whether the justification still applies to the current context.

**F2.** If any of the five rules is shown to currently serve its original rational purpose without the context having changed, that rule is not an instance of inherited enforcement. Evidence would include: the problem the rule was designed to address is still the primary problem, and the rule's current effect matches its intended effect.

**F3.** If the selection effect is shown to not operate — if cross-domain thinkers, instinctive falsifiers, and boundary-finders enter and advance in institutional science at the same rate as within-domain confirmers — the selection argument is not supported.

**F4.** If the institution is shown to have updated any of the five rules in response to the documented context changes — if falsification is rewarded equally with confirmation, if null results are published as first-class findings, if cross-domain credentials exist, if the replacement demand has been removed, if the patching mechanism has been replaced by immutable timestamps — then the institution has addressed that specific inherited enforcement and this paper's claim does not apply to that rule.

**F5.** If the institution demonstrates that it applies its own findings on inherited enforcement, institutional inertia, and groupthink to its own practices — that it systematically examines its own rules for outdated justifications and updates them when the context changes — then the studied blind spot described in Section VIII does not exist.

Each criterion is specific and stated before the evidence is examined.

## XII. BOUNDARIES AND LIMITATIONS

This paper describes a mechanism. It does not claim that every rule in institutional science is an instance of inherited enforcement. Some rules may still serve their original purpose. The paper identifies five specific rules and documents the context change for each. Other rules are not addressed.

This paper does not claim that individual scientists are malicious, incompetent, or aware of the enforcement they participate in. The mechanism operates through rational individuals making rational decisions within an inherited structure. The structure produces the outcome. The individuals are the mechanism, not the cause.

This paper does not prescribe reforms. It describes the cage. Whether and how to address the described mechanism is a decision for the people inside the cage and the people outside it. The paper provides the description. The decision is theirs.

The five monkeys experiment is a simplified model. Real institutional dynamics are more complex. The paper uses the model to identify the core mechanism — inherited enforcement — and traces its operation through documented structural features of institutional science. The model is a lens, not a complete theory of institutional behavior.

The companion PHYS and MATH papers are cited as existence proofs that the ladder can be climbed. Whether those papers' specific claims are correct is evaluated in their own reviews and falsification criteria.
