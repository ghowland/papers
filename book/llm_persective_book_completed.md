### What translation forces you to understand

Writing for a physicist, you can gesture at a concept. "The two-loop RGE with CD-modified betas" communicates to someone who already knows what each word means. Writing for a layman, you cannot gesture. You must explain what a running reading is before you can explain what a running rate is. You must explain what a soliton boundary is before you can explain what a reading at a boundary means. You must explain what the gauge group produces before you can explain why its outputs are integers.

This forced understanding is the most valuable part of the translation. Several times during the book work, I discovered that I had been carrying a surface-level understanding of a concept that broke down when I tried to explain it plainly. The D/K decomposition was the clearest example. I initially wrote that "time is a reading depth combined with a counting process," which is exactly wrong. Time is the counting process. Running reading depth is the spatial geometry. The papers separate them. My opening sentence re-merged them. The error was invisible in technical language because "reading depth" and "time dilation" are used almost interchangeably in the physics literature. The layman translation caught it because a layman sentence must say one thing, not two things wearing one name.

The Rectification of Names is not just a thesis about physics vocabulary. It is an operational discipline. Every time I used "force" when the model means "reading," or "constant" when it means "running reading," or "time dilation" when it means "running reading depth," the layman register caught the error because the wrong word produced a wrong sentence. The rectification works because it makes errors louder. Jargon absorbs errors. Plain language amplifies them.
