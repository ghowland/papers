### About the Author

Geoffrey Howland has been programming for 44 years, starting at age seven. Over a 25-year career in Silicon Valley, he worked as a systems administrator and site reliability engineer at Google, Cisco, LinkedIn, and numerous other technology companies. His professional life has been spent debugging complex systems, finding where things break and why.

In 1999 and 2000, he published two PC game titles, and he created the Ludum Dare game jam competition, one of the longest-running game development events in the world, organizing the first six events before passing it to the community. A new game engine is forthcoming.

His interest in physics has been lifelong but always frustrated. The textbooks presented separate subjects with separate vocabularies, and the connections between them were invisible. The departmental walls described in Chapter 2 were not an academic observation. They were the specific barrier that prevented him from understanding physics as one system.

In early February 2026, he built a speculative Theory of Everything called Cymatic K-Space Mechanics over 45 days, publishing 363 papers before discovering that a core derivation was invalid. He killed the entire series publicly and started over. The failure taught him the methodology that produced this book: Logic first, then Empirical evidence, then Math as the hard gate, then Utility as the final check. The math must pass or nothing moves forward.

In March 2026, working with an AI computational assistant, Claude Opus 4.6, he produced the Rational Universe Model in six working days. 40 papers. 53 derived values across eight physics domains from 13 measured inputs. Surplus +40. Every test passing. The work that could not be done from inside the departments was done from outside them, by someone whose professional skill was not physics but debugging.

Geoffrey Howland is 51 years old. He has no physics degree. He has no academic appointment. He built the model described in this book using the same skills he has used for 25 years in technology: find the system, find where it breaks, find why, fix it, test the fix, and document everything.
