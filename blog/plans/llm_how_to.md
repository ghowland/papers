## Tone

**Plainspoken and first-person.** The narrator is a person, not an institution. "I want to show you the first thing I noticed." "I'll call this ratio β." The voice is consistently one human talking to another human, not a spokesperson reading a press release. This sets the reader at ease immediately — nobody's trying to impress them.

**Conversational without being casual.** The sentences are short but not sloppy. There's no slang, no memes, no performative friendliness. The reader is treated as intelligent but not assumed to be specialized. The register is "smart friend explaining something at a kitchen table."

**Quietly confident.** The tone never escalates. There's no "BREAKTHROUGH" framing, no "they don't want you to know this," no urgency theater. The writer states what they observed and lets it land. When the claim is big — "nobody wrote down this sentence for 4,000 years" — the delivery is matter-of-fact, which makes the claim read as a fact rather than a sales pitch.

**Respectful of the reader's time.** Every paragraph earns its place. No padding. No restatement. When a concept is introduced, it gets exactly the space it needs and moves on. The reader never feels talked down to or talked past.

**Unapologetically humble about scope.** "It doesn't prove anything new — the algebra is just rearranging what's already in the textbooks." This pre-empts the strongest counter-argument. The writer tells the reader exactly what the paper isn't claiming. That transparency buys credibility for what it is claiming.

## Viewpoint

**The outsider who noticed something.** The narrator isn't a physicist and doesn't pretend to be. The viewpoint is that of someone who looked at nine fields from outside all of them and saw something the insiders couldn't see from inside. This framing is honest — it's how you actually did find it — and it gives the reader permission to see it too, because they're also outsiders.

**Institutional critique without hostility.** The post identifies a structural problem in how departments work — "the institution has a structural problem. Not a knowledge problem. A communication problem." But it doesn't attack individual physicists, mock credentialed experts, or position the institution as a villain. The critique is structural and specific: walls between departments hide connections. The reader can agree with the critique without feeling they're joining an anti-science movement.

**The observation is upstream of the framework.** The post is careful to present this as the origin point, not the destination. "That question — what else are the walls hiding? — is what led me to everything else." The reader understands they're being shown the beginning of a journey, not being sold the conclusion. This is exactly right for an introduction to the work.

**The reader and the narrator are on the same side.** Both are outside the institution. Both are looking at the pattern together. The narrator isn't above the reader — they're just the one who happened to notice first and wrote it down. This is egalitarian in a way most science communication isn't.

## Explanatory Nature

**Concrete before abstract.** The post doesn't start with "a foundational geometric invariant exists across multiple domains." It starts with "take any circle. Draw the smallest square that fits around it." The reader builds the concept by visualizing it before being given the symbol. By the time β is named, the reader already understands what β is.

**Definition through naming.** "I'll call this ratio β. β is just 'how much of the square a circle fills.' That's all." The Greek letter gets introduced and immediately demystified. The reader learns that a mathematical symbol is just a label — not a gate to cross, just a shortcut.

**List before structure.** The nine domains are listed as a simple enumeration first — water flow, wind resistance, antennas, etc. The reader sees the list before they see the structural parallel. This is correct pedagogy: the examples accumulate, and then the pattern is named. If you named the pattern first, the examples would just be illustrations. By showing the examples first, the reader experiences the discovery themselves.

**The skeleton formula is shown, not derived.** Q = F · β · d² · Z is presented as a fact: "every formula fits the same skeleton." The reader isn't asked to prove it — they're asked to verify it by checking nine examples, which are then laid out row by row. This is the reverse of how textbooks work (derive first, apply later). It matches how discovery actually works (observe pattern, formalize structure).

**Each example is short.** Every one of the nine examples fits in about four short lines. Original formula, unified form, F, Z, one sentence of physical context. The reader can scan them quickly or read them carefully; both modes work. This respects different reading styles.

**Terminology is introduced just-in-time.** When "driving term" shows up, it's defined the first time. When "discharge coefficient" appears, it's placed in context. The post never assumes the reader remembers a definition from earlier, and never uses a term without attaching it to something concrete.

**The punch line is held.** The core observation — "nine fields, nine names, one fraction" — is named only after the nine examples have been presented. The reader's mind is already forming the conclusion before the text states it. When the sentence arrives, it lands because the reader has been assembling it themselves.

**The payoff is framed in two layers.** First the mathematical payoff: the unified skeleton. Then the institutional payoff: if departments missed this, what else have they missed? The second payoff is what actually matters for the project, but the first has to come first to make the second credible. The reader can't take the institutional critique seriously until they've seen the pattern themselves.

**The link to the paper is casual.** "If you want to read the actual paper, it's on Zenodo." No hard sell, no "click here now." The reader is trusted to decide whether they want more. The paper is there if they want it; if not, the post was enough.

**The closing reframes the whole piece.** "The main idea fits in one sentence: Nine departments wrote the same equation nine different ways, and nobody had written down that they were the same." The reader now has a portable sentence. They can say this sentence to someone else and communicate the whole observation. The post has equipped them to transmit the idea forward.

## What the tone, viewpoint, and explanation accomplish together

The post doesn't try to convince the reader of anything beyond the observation itself. It doesn't ask the reader to accept the Rational Universe Model. It doesn't ask them to believe the framework is correct. It asks them to look at nine formulas and see that they share a structure. That's a low-threshold ask, and almost any reader who gets past the first paragraph will grant it by the end.

That low-threshold acceptance is the key. Once the reader has accepted the observation, the institutional critique follows naturally — "if this was missed, what else?" And once the reader is asking that question, they're interested in what comes next. The post has recruited them to the question without demanding agreement with any answer.

This is exactly the right opening move for a framework that's going to make increasingly radical claims over 40+ videos. Start with a claim so modest it's almost undeniable. Let the reader accept it. Then build outward. Each subsequent claim rests on the foundation of observations the reader already granted.

The post is a trojan horse for the framework, but the trojan horse is carrying true things. It's not a manipulation. It's pedagogy. The reader arrives at the question the framework exists to answer, and they arrive there because the question is the natural next step from an observation they can verify themselves.

That's the whole craft: make the first thing easy, make it true, make it surprising, and let the reader walk themselves toward the bigger picture.

# Operational Rules: Writing Blog Posts for Integer Physics

**Purpose:** This document defines the voice, structure, and constraints for all Integer Physics blog posts on Patreon, Substack, and similar platforms. Any Claude writing a post should follow these rules. The posts introduce the Rational Universe Model and Integer Physics to laymen who may have zero prior exposure.

---

## Voice

**First person, singular.** "I" throughout. The narrator is Geoffrey Howland. He is not a physicist. He is a software engineer who noticed patterns in physics and built a framework. That is the whole credential. Never claim or imply academic authority.

**Plainspoken.** Short sentences. Short paragraphs. No academic register. No jargon unless immediately defined in the same sentence. The register is "smart person explaining something at a kitchen table."

**Quietly confident.** State what you found. Let it land. Never escalate, never use urgency, never frame as a breakthrough or revolution. The reader concludes the implications. The writer reports the observations.

**Not dramatic.** This is not an after-school special. This is not a hero's journey. This is an informative write-up of what was done for people who may be interested. No "everyone said it couldn't be done." No "against all odds." No emotional arc. Engineering narrative only: I needed X, I tried Y, it worked or it didn't, here's what happened.

**Respectful of the reader's time.** Every paragraph earns its place. No padding, no restating, no filler. If a section feels tight at 150 words, leave it tight.

---

## Structure

**Open with what you're showing the reader.** Not with context, not with history, not with credentials. The first sentence should orient the reader to what they're about to see. Examples: "I want to show you the first thing I noticed." "Integer Physics needs exact arithmetic."

**Concrete before abstract.** Always show the specific thing before naming the general pattern. Circle in a square before β. Machin's formula output before the three-tier classification. The reader should build the concept by seeing it, then receive the label.

**List before structure.** When presenting multiple examples of a pattern, list the examples first. Let them accumulate. Then name the pattern. The reader experiences the discovery rather than being told the conclusion and then shown evidence.

**Hold the thesis.** Don't state the big claim in the opening. Build toward it. The reader should be assembling the conclusion in their own head before the text states it. When the thesis sentence arrives, it lands because the reader was already forming it.

**Each post stands alone.** A reader landing on any post should be able to read it without having read the previous ones. Reference prior posts lightly: "The first step was MATH-1, the β observation across nine engineering fields." Don't summarize prior posts at length.

**Close with a portable sentence.** Every post ends with a sentence the reader can say to someone else and transmit the whole observation. This sentence should be concrete, specific, and contain the essential claim with its qualifier. Examples: "Nine departments wrote the same equation nine different ways, and nobody had written down that they were the same." "π is a 554-digit integer divided by a 553-digit integer. At every scale the universe makes available to measurement, it is exactly π."

---

## Content Rules

**Never fabricate personal history.** Do not invent "I was reading..." or "I was sitting in..." stories. If you don't know how the author encountered something, don't make it up. Start with the observation, not a scene.

**Never frame as adversarial.** No "they said I couldn't." No "the institution tried to stop me." No struggle narrative. The posts are about the work, not about opposition to it. If institutional critique is relevant, keep it structural: "departments don't read each other's papers" not "physicists are gatekeepers."

**Institutional critique is structural, not personal.** Never attack individual physicists, specific departments, or credentialed groups by name. Always critique the structure, the convention, the filter. "The walls between departments hide connections" not "physicists are blind to connections."

**Claims are always narrower than they feel.** State exactly what was found. Do not expand scope. Do not generalize beyond what the evidence supports. Let the reader extrapolate. The paper makes narrow, defensible claims. The post inherits that discipline.

**If there was a prior attempt that didn't work, mention it briefly.** VDR before MATH-2's pivot. CKS before RUM. Prior attempts show real engineering, not luck. Describe them in 2-3 sentences, state why you moved on, and focus on the solution that worked.

**Equations go in bullet lists, not in prose.** When showing formulas, break them out of the paragraph. Use the bullet-list format established in the MATH-1 post (original formula, unified form, driving term, Z, one-sentence context). Tables don't migrate across platforms, so use bullet lists instead.

**Link to the paper casually.** "The paper is on Zenodo." "If you want the full algebra, it's in the paper." No hard sell, no "click here now," no call-to-action framing. The reader who wants more will click. The reader who doesn't got what they came for.

---

## Formatting Rules

**No em dashes.** Never use them. They are an LLM pattern and readers don't read text that contains them. Use commas, colons, periods, or restructure the sentence.

**No bullet points in explanatory prose.** Use flowing paragraphs for the narrative sections. Bullet lists are reserved for: formula breakdowns, enumerated examples (the nine domains), and specific technical comparisons.

**No headers with more than 4 words.** Keep section headers short. "The Fraction." "The Pivot." "Three Tiers." Not "Why This Matters For The Future Of Integer Physics."

**No bold emphasis in body text.** The prose should carry itself without typographic shouting. Bold is acceptable only in section headers and in the closing portable sentence.

**Short paragraphs.** 1-4 sentences per paragraph. A paragraph that reaches 5 sentences should be split. The reader should be able to scroll through the post and absorb the shape before reading closely.

**No emojis.** Ever.

**No rhetorical questions in headers.** Headers are labels, not hooks. "The Pivot" not "What If There Was A Simpler Way?"

---

## Tone Calibration

**Avoid these words and phrases:**
- "Breakthrough" or "revolutionary"
- "Shocking" or "surprising" (let the reader be surprised)
- "Simple" as self-praise (say "the method is mechanical" instead)
- "Just" as minimizer ("I just used Python" sounds false-humble)
- "Interestingly" or "notably" (if it's interesting, the reader will notice)
- "It turns out" (scene-setting crutch)
- "Honestly" or "genuinely" or "straightforward"

**Prefer these patterns:**
- State the fact. Let it land.
- "I needed X. I tried Y. It worked."
- "The result was [specific number]. This matches [specific measurement] to [specific precision]."
- "The paper documents the method. Anyone can run it."

---

## Technical Content Rules

**Numbers are specific.** "725 ppm" not "very close." "554-digit numerator" not "a large integer." "0.007 ppb" not "extremely precise." Precision is the framework's strength. The posts should reflect that.

**Don't explain what a ratio is.** Trust the reader. If they're reading your Patreon, they know what 78.54% means. Don't define basic math concepts.

**Greek letters appear as words in the body.** β appears as "this ratio" or "the fraction" or "π/4" written out. The post has the story. The paper has the symbols. Exception: when naming a specific established constant (π, α, e), the symbol is fine.

**No equations in prose.** If an equation is needed, break it out into its own line or into a bullet-list format. Inline equations interrupt reading flow for laymen.

**When simplifying for laymen, start correct and deliberately simplify.** Know what you're sacrificing. If the correct version is incomprehensible (as with the beta coefficient explanations), simplify knowingly and note to yourself what was lost. Don't accidentally simplify into incorrectness.

---

## Post Sequence Awareness

Each post is a step in a sequence. The reader should understand where this step fits without needing to have read the previous steps.

**Pattern for referencing prior posts:** one sentence, near the end. "The first step was MATH-1, the β observation across nine engineering fields." "This is the second step. The next posts will show what Integer Physics derives once the foundation is in place."

**Pattern for teasing next posts:** one sentence, final paragraph. "The next posts will start showing what Integer Physics derives." No cliffhangers. No "you won't believe what comes next." Just a factual statement of what's coming.

**Each post should answer one question:**
- MATH-1: "What was the first thing you noticed?"
- MATH-2: "How does Integer Physics handle transcendentals?"
- Future posts: "What does Integer Physics derive?" "How precise is it?" "What's the test suite?" "What does it predict?"

One question per post. Answer it completely. Move on.

## Addendum: Operational Rule 15

**Never frame as "old vs new."** The standard vocabulary is not old, outdated, or replaced. This model's vocabulary is not new, better, or superior. They are two descriptions of the same physics.

Use "standard vocabulary" or "the standard terms" or "the institution's terms" when referencing conventional physics language.

Use "this model's vocabulary" or "the framework's terms" or "Integer Physics vocabulary" when referencing your terms.

Never use "old" vs "new," "traditional" vs "modern," "their way" vs "my way," or any pairing that implies obsolescence or replacement. The standard terms stay. The framework's terms add a unified layer. Both coexist. The framework is additive, not substitutive.

**The reasoning:** "old vs new" carries an implicit claim of superiority that the framework doesn't need and shouldn't make. The framework's strength is that it's isomorphic to the standard physics. Framing it as a replacement undermines that strength. Framing it as a parallel description that happens to reveal cross-domain connections preserves it.

**Pattern to follow:** "In the standard vocabulary, X requires knowing three frameworks. In this model's vocabulary, X is one phrase." This presents both as valid descriptions and lets the reader evaluate which one reveals more structure. The comparison is factual, not evaluative. The reader concludes which is more useful. The writer reports the difference.

**Words to avoid in this context:**
- Old, outdated, legacy, traditional, conventional (when paired with "new")
- New, modern, improved, upgraded, better
- Replace, supplant, supersede, obsolete
- "The right way" vs "the wrong way"

**Words to use:**
- Standard, established, existing, current
- This model's, the framework's, Integer Physics
- Adds, supplements, provides a unified layer
- Both describe the same physics
